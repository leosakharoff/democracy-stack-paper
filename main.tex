% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Europe is investing in democratic digital infrastructure, but there is little guidance on what good software architecture looks like when the goal is democracy rather than service delivery. Architecture shapes governance --- this idea is well established in science and technology studies (STS) and platform studies --- yet to the best of our knowledge, no study has applied software architecture or software ecosystem methods to civic technology platforms. This paper addresses that gap through a multi-case analysis of three platforms: Decidim (deep case with empirical architectural recovery from gemspec files and the commit history), Consul (comparison case --- shared origin with Decidim, opposite architecture, with fork-divergence analysis across 1{,}030 GitHub forks), and Polis (contrasting algorithmic paradigm). The analysis surfaces three architectural trade-offs that function as governance decisions: modifiability against sustainability burden, interoperability against reduced autonomy, and algorithmic depth against ecosystem breadth. The two-platform comparison provides the strongest empirical anchor for the architecture-as-governance thesis. The paper brings software architecture and ecosystem research into the European policy debate about building a ``democracy stack.''
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Europe is investing in democratic digital infrastructure: the Digital Services Act, the European Digital Identity framework, International IDEA's ``democracy stack'' framing \citep{idea2026democracy}, CEPS's interoperability advocacy \citep{ceps2025building}. Neither the policy framing nor the civic tech research literature specifies what good architecture looks like when the goal is democracy rather than service delivery.

The gap is in the methods, not the field. Civic tech platforms (Decidim, Consul, Polis among them) have been building democratic infrastructure for over a decade. Software architecture research offers frameworks for analysing quality attributes and trade-offs \citep{bass2021software}; SECO research offers frameworks for ecosystem health and governance \citep{manikas2016revisiting, christensen2014analysis}. Neither has been applied to civic technology. \citet{knutas2020local} proposed applying open-source ecosystem analysis to civic tech, but the empirical work was not done. \citet{palacin2024configurations} come closest, studying how Decidim's modularity produces 25 distinct configurations and asking which political values should be enforced by platform design, but without performing architectural analysis.

This paper asks: \emph{what software architecture trade-offs must builders of democratic digital infrastructure navigate?}

We address this through a multi-case architectural decision analysis of three platforms: Decidim (deep case with empirical architectural recovery), Consul (comparison case with shared origin to Decidim, opposite architecture, fork-divergence analysis across all enumerable GitHub forks), and Polis (contrasting algorithmic paradigm). The Decidim/Consul divergence is the central evidentiary move: a shared starting point, opposite architectural decisions, very different ecosystems. Architectural decisions in civic tech function as governance decisions; this paper provides the kind of architectural analysis IDEA's ``democracy stack'' framework calls for but does not itself deliver.

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Software Architecture for Democratic Infrastructure}\label{sec:sa}

Software architecture is ``the set of structures needed to reason about a system'' \citep[p.~2]{bass2021software}. Architecture decisions shape what a system can and cannot do over its lifetime through quality attributes: measurable properties like modifiability, interoperability, availability, deployability, and testability. In commercial software these are optimised for business value; when the system is democratic infrastructure, the same reasoning applies with a different goal: which architecture best supports democratic participation and public legitimacy?

This paper focuses on three architectural attributes where civic tech platforms most clearly diverge. \emph{Modifiability} is how easily a system can be changed; it matters because communities with different institutional rules, languages, and participatory traditions need to adapt the platform without hard-forking the codebase. \emph{Interoperability} is how well a system can exchange data and services with others, determining whether it can work with other democratic tools or stays isolated. \emph{Algorithmic depth} is how much of the platform's democratic value comes from non-trivial algorithms (statistical models, machine-learning components) rather than structural composition; deep-algorithm platforms can solve problems purely structural ones cannot, but tighten internal coupling and narrow the contributor base. Deployability and transparency surface in the cases as descriptive observations (Polis is Docker-easy and opaque; Decidim's 27-engine setup is complex but readable), not as primary axes.

Each attribute pulls against a cost: modifiability against the sustainability burden of many composable parts; interoperability against reduced local autonomy under shared standards; algorithmic depth against ecosystem breadth. Each also maps to a democratic outcome: pluralism (diverse communities can adapt the platform), collaboration (platforms compose into wider stacks), and computational consensus (deliberation that scales beyond what unaided humans can synthesise). These are design trade-offs not problems to solve \citep{heeks2025three}, and the mappings to democratic outcomes are reasoned arguments not proven causal links. Figure~\ref{fig:tradeoffs} positions the three cases on two of these tensions; the third is summarised in Table~\ref{tab:tradeoffs}.

\subsection{Software Ecosystems and Civic Tech}\label{sec:seco}

A software ecosystem (SECO) is ``the interaction of a set of actors on top of a common technological platform, which results in a number of software solutions or services'' \citep{manikas2013systematic}. The concept extends beyond a single product to the organisations, developers, and users that develop around a shared platform.

\citet{christensen2014analysis} propose that ecosystems can be analysed through three structures: \emph{organisational}, \emph{business}, and \emph{software}. In commercial ecosystems the business structure usually dominates (Apple's revenue model shapes what apps get built). We hypothesise that in civic tech it works differently: the software structure shapes the other two. Christensen et al.\ do not claim any fixed ordering, but the pattern recurs across all three of our cases (Table~\ref{tab:threestructure}).

\citet{iansiti2004strategy} classify ecosystem actors as \emph{keystones}, \emph{dominators}, and \emph{niche creators}; we return to how the three cases fit these roles in Section~\ref{sec:disc-gov}. We also draw on ecosystem health dimensions \citep{jansen2014measuring} --- productivity, robustness, niche creation --- as qualitative lenses in that discussion.

Ecosystem thinking is clearly useful for civic tech, but the literature has a gap. \citet{manikas2016revisiting}'s systematic review covered 231 studies and found none on commons-based, civic, or democratic platforms; \citet{knutas2020local} proposed applying the OSSECO framework to civic tech, but the empirical work was never done. The same gap exists on the software architecture side: the architecture-as-governance thesis \citep{winner1980artifacts, lessig2006code, schneider2024governable, denardis2009protocol} is well established but has not been applied to civic technology platforms using software architecture methods. \citet[p.~68]{barandiaran2024decidim} note that ``Decidim must take into account the democratic implications of any technical decision''; \citet{palacin2024configurations} study how Decidim's modularity produces 25 distinct configurations and ask which political values should be enforced by platform design, but without architectural analysis. This paper fills both gaps.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

This study uses a multi-case architectural decision analysis backed by empirical recovery from each platform's source repository. We focus on the design decisions that shaped each platform and trace their effects on quality attributes relevant to democratic infrastructure. For each case the method combines four kinds of empirical work: architectural recovery from gemspec and source files, evolutionary analysis from version control history, logical coupling analysis across modules, and fork divergence analysis where it applies. The cases are treated with intentional depth asymmetry: Decidim is the deep case (full architectural recovery, per-engine evolutionary analysis, logical coupling); Consul is the comparison case (lighter recovery, evolutionary analysis, and fork divergence across 1{,}030 GitHub forks); Polis is included as a contrasting paradigm with lighter analysis. The Decidim/Consul pair briefly shared early code; their architectural split approximates (though does not constitute) a natural experiment, shaped by organisational and political factors as well as technical ones \citep{barandiaran2024decidim}, which we revisit in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}. Reproducibility scripts and findings logs are kept alongside the paper source.

\paragraph{Case selection.}
The three cases were selected using two criteria. First, a publicly developed codebase, an active or recently active deployment, and enough documentation for architectural recovery --- this rules out closed platforms like borgerforslag.dk, thin demonstrators, and undocumented platforms. Second, variation along the architectural dimension we want to study: monolithic versus modular versus algorithmic. Decidim and Consul share an origin in Spain's 15M movement (the comparison's empirical anchor); Polis adds an algorithmically driven platform with a different participation paradigm to test whether the trade-off framework holds. Other widely deployed civic tech platforms (Loomio, CitizenLab, vTaiwan, Adhocracy, LiquidFeedback) are not analysed here; the three were sufficient to identify the trade-offs without exhausting the field. We return to this scoping in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}.

\paragraph{Architectural recovery.}
For each platform we recover the architecture from primary sources: codebase structure, gemspec files, extension mechanisms, and documentation. For Decidim, a script parses all 27 first-party gemspecs at a pinned snapshot, extracts every \texttt{add\_dependency} call, classifies each as internal or external, and identifies \texttt{Decidim.register\_component} and \texttt{Decidim.register\_participatory\_space} calls. The engine dependency graph in Section~\ref{sec:decidim} is recovered from this data, not asserted from documentation. Consul's recovery is structural (a Rails monolith with a file-override extension mechanism; see Section~\ref{sec:consul}); Polis recovery focuses on the data pipeline rather than module structure, since the algorithm is the architectural unit (Section~\ref{sec:polis}).

\paragraph{Evolutionary analysis.}
For all three platforms we extract commit metadata with \texttt{git log --no-merges} on partial clones. Bot authors (Dependabot, Crowdin, Weblate, GitHub Actions, named release bots) are filtered by email and name patterns. We compute total human commits, unique authors, top-three and top-ten contributor share, Gini coefficient, and the per-year commit timeline; for Decidim we also extract per-engine commit counts. These serve as ecosystem-health indicators \citep{manikas2013systematic, jansen2014measuring}.

\paragraph{Logical coupling.}
Following \citet{gall1998detection}, we measure which Decidim engines change together. For each non-merge non-bot commit we identify the engines touched and compute Jaccard similarity (commits touching both / commits touching either) for each engine pair. Mega-commits touching more than eight engines are excluded as refactor or release commits that would otherwise dominate the signal. This tests whether the manifest pattern produces isolation at the change level, not only at the gemspec level.

\paragraph{Fork divergence.}
For Consul we use the GitHub REST API to enumerate forks of \texttt{consuldemocracy/consuldemocracy} (formerly \texttt{consul/consul}) and call the compare endpoint for each, returning commits ahead and behind upstream master. Each fork is classified as \emph{modified} or \emph{unmodified} (commits ahead) and \emph{active} or \emph{dormant} (pushed in the last two years). Decidim is not subject to this analysis (its deployers use gem plugins, not forks); Polis's fork count is too small to be informative.

\paragraph{Trade-off scope.}
We bring three architectural tensions to the cases, specified up front from the architecture-as-governance literature rather than induced from the empirical material: \emph{modifiability against sustainability burden}, \emph{interoperability against reduced autonomy}, and \emph{algorithmic depth against ecosystem breadth}. Each pairs a quality attribute civic tech platforms compete on against an architectural cost the same attribute imposes. \citet{bass2021software} list many more attributes; security, availability, deployability, and transparency surface in the cases as descriptive observations but not as primary axes. For each case we identify the architectural decisions that visibly trade off between these tensions (modular versus monolithic, plugin versus fork, shared versus isolated data, algorithm-as-product versus algorithm-as-feature), drawing on the ADR tradition \citep{tyree2005architecture} for structure without producing formal ADRs.

\paragraph{Data sources and limitations.}
Primary sources are the public GitHub repositories at pinned snapshots: \texttt{decidim/decidim} at \texttt{be39c244} (2026-05-12), \texttt{consuldemocracy/consuldemocracy} at \texttt{3c3b5c4a} (2026-05-11), \texttt{compdemocracy/polis} at \texttt{e8c2b46d} (2026-04-26). Secondary sources include \citet{barandiaran2024decidim} on Decidim's sociotechnical design, the OSOR case study on Consul's European deployments \citep{linaker2025consul}, the NTARI fork report \citep{ntari2025consul}, and the Codegram architecture blog on early Decidim design decisions \citep{codegram-decidim}. The main limitation, expanded in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}, is that our quality-attribute-to-democracy mappings are analytical, not empirically validated. Architectural recovery captures dependency structure, not runtime behaviour; evolutionary metrics use email-based author identity, which conflates authors with multiple emails; fork analysis covers public forks only.

\section{Analysis}\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Decidim: Architecture Recovery}\label{sec:decidim}

Decidim is a modular monolith built with Ruby on Rails. The monorepo at \url{github.com/decidim/decidim} contains 27 first-party engines packaged as standalone Ruby gems --- 24 user-facing platform engines and three developer-tooling engines (\texttt{design}, \texttt{dev}, \texttt{generators}). This section recovers the architecture from the codebase and documentation, focusing on the decisions that shaped the platform's trajectory. Recovery is pinned to commit \texttt{be39c244} (2026-05-12).

\paragraph{Engine structure.}
The 24 user-facing engines fall into four groups: \texttt{decidim-core} as the foundational dependency; three infrastructure engines (\texttt{admin}, \texttt{api}, \texttt{system}); three shared-service engines other engines depend on (\texttt{comments}, \texttt{forms}, \texttt{verifications}); four participatory spaces as containers (\texttt{processes}, \texttt{assemblies}, \texttt{conferences}, \texttt{initiatives}); and 13 components that attach to those spaces (\texttt{proposals}, \texttt{meetings}, \texttt{budgets}, \texttt{debates}, \texttt{accountability}, \texttt{surveys}, \texttt{blogs}, \texttt{pages}, \texttt{elections}, \texttt{templates}, \texttt{collaborative\_texts}, \texttt{demographics}, \texttt{ai}). The last three are recent additions visible in the commit history; \texttt{decidim-sortitions} existed in earlier versions but was removed on 2025-11-19.

\paragraph{Dependency graph.}
Every engine except \texttt{decidim-core} itself declares \texttt{add\_dependency "decidim-core"} in its gemspec, matching the textbook hub-and-spoke pattern. But 13 of the 27 engines also have runtime dependencies on other Decidim engines besides core. \texttt{decidim-comments} has 6 dependents (\texttt{accountability}, \texttt{blogs}, \texttt{budgets}, \texttt{debates}, \texttt{initiatives}, \texttt{proposals}); \texttt{decidim-forms} has 5 (\texttt{demographics}, \texttt{elections}, \texttt{meetings}, \texttt{surveys}, \texttt{templates}). These two are de-facto Layer-2 hubs of the platform rather than peers of the infrastructure engines. One cross-engine dependency is architecturally notable: \texttt{decidim-conferences} (a space) runtime-depends on \texttt{decidim-meetings} (a component), so the conferences space hard-requires meetings rather than treating it as an optional plugin; we return to this below. Figure~\ref{fig:dependency} shows the full graph, recovered from the gemspec files.

\paragraph{The space/component matrix.}
This is the key architectural pattern. \citet[p.~82]{barandiaran2024decidim} compare participatory components to furniture: an administrator can mix and match features (proposals, budgets, surveys, debates) with participation contexts (processes, assemblies, conferences) without writing code. Almost any component can attach to almost any space, creating an $N \times M$ combinatorial matrix \citep[p.~75]{barandiaran2024decidim} implemented through a shared database table in \texttt{decidim-core}. Figure~\ref{fig:matrix} illustrates this composition. The pattern is mostly clean, with one exception flagged above: \texttt{decidim-conferences} hard-requires \texttt{decidim-meetings} at the gem level.

\paragraph{Four interaction mechanisms.}
The engines interact through four mechanisms that define the internal API boundaries:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \emph{Rails engine mounting.} Each engine uses \texttt{isolate\_namespace}; component engines mount dynamically under the space's URL scope at runtime.
  \item \emph{Manifest and registry pattern.} \texttt{decidim-core} maintains global registries (\texttt{ComponentManifest}, \texttt{ParticipatorySpaceManifest}). Engines register themselves at boot by calling \texttt{Decidim.register\_component} or \texttt{Decidim.register\_participatory\_space}; core reads from these registries to build menus, routes, and the API.
  \item \emph{Shared database.} All engines write to one PostgreSQL database; cross-engine data access happens through ActiveRecord associations, not service calls. Tight data coupling without distributed-system overhead.
  \item \emph{Pub/sub events.} \texttt{Decidim::EventsManager.publish} wraps \texttt{ActiveSupport::Notifications}, letting modules trigger cross-engine side effects (e.g.\ notifying followers when a proposal is published) without direct dependencies.
\end{enumerate}

Listing~\ref{lst:register} shows the manifest pattern concretely --- the interface both first-party and third-party modules use.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Simplified Decidim component registration (based on \texttt{decidim-proposals}).}, label=lst:register, language=Ruby]
# decidim-proposals/lib/decidim/proposals/engine.rb
Decidim.register_component(:proposals) do |component|
  component.engine = Decidim::Proposals::Engine
  component.admin_engine = Decidim::Proposals::AdminEngine
  component.icon = "media/images/decidim_proposals.svg"

  # Declare what this component can do

  end

    settings.attribute :vote_limit, type: :integer, default: 0
    settings.attribute :minimum_votes_per_user, type: :integer
  end
end
\end{lstlisting}

\paragraph{Module isolation in practice.}
The gemspec dependencies describe what the engines \emph{can} do, not what they actually do over time. To test whether modularity is matched by modular evolution, we apply Gall et al.'s logical coupling analysis \citep{gall1998detection} to the commit history (procedure in Section~\ref{sec:method}). Of the 5{,}517 commits that touched at least one engine, $64\%$ stay inside a single engine --- the architecture provides real, not nominal, isolation. The remaining cross-engine coupling is heavily clustered (Figure~\ref{fig:coupling}). Three patterns stand out. First, the four participatory spaces co-change at Jaccard up to $0.43$ despite having no gemspec dependencies between them; the ``space'' abstraction is unified at the source level, not implemented as four independent realisations of a clean interface. Second, the flagship components (\texttt{proposals}, \texttt{meetings}, \texttt{budgets}, \texttt{debates}) co-evolve at $J = 0.11$--$0.20$ because real deployments use them together. Third, and counter-intuitively, engines that gemspec-depend on \texttt{decidim-comments} or \texttt{decidim-forms} barely co-change with them ($J < 0.06$ in most cases): the shared services have stable APIs, and the manifest pattern works as advertised for API consumption. The modularity is at API boundaries, not at implementation.

\paragraph{Third-party extension mechanism.}
Community modules use the same interface as first-party ones: a Ruby gem that depends on \texttt{decidim-core} and calls \texttt{Decidim.register\_component} in an initialiser. Installation is one line in the deployer's \texttt{Gemfile}; Rails engine loading does the rest. This design has produced over 80 community-developed modules that work alongside the core engines.

\paragraph{Quality attribute profile.}
\emph{Modifiability} is high. The engine architecture lets deployers add, remove, or replace components without touching core code; customisation happens through modules, not forks. \emph{Interoperability} is moderate: a GraphQL API for data access and deliberately open-source integration choices (OpenStreetMap, Matomo, Jitsi), but no cross-platform standards for civic tech. Deployability is moderate-to-low (a Rails application with the full engine set; multi-tenancy helps), and transparency is high by design: proposals sorted randomly to prevent rich-get-richer dynamics, administrators cannot delete proposals, ``participant experience'' framing \citep{aragon2017deliberative}. All code is licensed under AGPL~v3.

\paragraph{Ecosystem indicators.}
As of May 2026, \texttt{decidim/decidim} has 1{,}748 stars and 469 forks on GitHub. The platform is deployed in over 450 institutions across 30 countries; Barcelona alone has over 120{,}000 registered participants. The technical office consists of only three employees, and the project is critically dependent on Catalan institutional funding \citep{barandiaran2024decidim}. The commit history gives a sharper picture: 7{,}078 human commits across 183 unique human authors since 2016, top-three share $36\%$, top-ten $68\%$, Gini $0.85$. \citet{cobos2025comparison} call this activity that ``tends to concentrate on a few contributors''; Section~\ref{sec:synthesis} shows Decidim is actually the most distributed of the three cases. Figure~\ref{fig:decidim-engine-activity} plots per-engine commits over the last 12 months: all 24 user-facing engines see activity, with three new engines (\texttt{ai}, \texttt{demographics}, \texttt{collaborative\_texts}) accumulating their first hundreds. The modular architecture is matched by modular maintenance; no engine is dormant. The ecosystem is productive and diverse, its robustness fragile.

\subsection{Consul: The Comparison Case}\label{sec:consul}

Consul and Decidim share an origin in Spain's 15M/Indignados movement. Barcelona initially contributed to Consul (which powered decide.madrid.es) but proposed modularising the codebase. According to the Codegram architecture blog, ``the talks on this document didn't come to a successful end'' \citep{codegram-decidim}; Barcelona then rewrote from scratch, creating Decidim in 2016--2017. The project's GitHub home has since been renamed from \texttt{consul/consul} to \texttt{consuldemocracy/consuldemocracy}.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
Consul is a single monolithic Rails application: no gem separation, no engine isolation. Features (proposals, debates, budgets, legislation, voting, polls) share the same codebase, the same \texttt{ApplicationController}, and one \texttt{config/routes.rb}. Models live in \texttt{app/models/} with functional subdirectories (e.g.\ \texttt{budget/investment.rb}), but with no module-level namespace boundaries enforced --- a budget model can freely reference a proposal model without going through any API. All tables sit in one schema. There is no \texttt{ARCHITECTURE.md} and no \texttt{Gemfile} separation; the technical documentation covers installation but not architecture.

\paragraph{Extension mechanism.}
Consul uses a ``custom shadow folder'' pattern: to change how a budget investment works, you create a file at the same path under \texttt{app/models/custom/} that overrides the original. This is monkey-patching, not module isolation. Customisation requires forking the entire repository --- the documentation's ``Getting Started'' step~1 is literally ``Create your fork.'' Deployers then maintain that fork across upgrades manually, resolving conflicts between their custom overrides and upstream changes every release. A \texttt{Gemfile\_custom} convention exists for added dependencies, but these load into the same process.

\paragraph{The fork problem.}
The OSOR/Interoperable Europe case study found ``fragmented development, with multiple forks adapted to different local needs\ldots\ difficult to synchronise updates and maintain security across installations'' \citep{linaker2025consul}, and that ``some expressed the belief that Consul felt like it was being forked by each local government\ldots\ with little getting contributed back upstream.'' We enumerated all forks via the GitHub API and computed each fork's divergence against upstream master. Of 1{,}030 enumerable forks (1{,}123 reported), 746 ($73\%$) are unmodified ``stars in fork form''; 280 ($27\%$) have at least one commit ahead --- approximately the ``around 250 deployments'' figure previous reports cite \citep{ntari2025consul}. The distribution within those 280 is heavily skewed: 147 have fewer than ten commits ahead, 39 have 100 or more, only 5 have 1{,}000 or more. The ``deployer maintains a fully separate codebase'' picture applies to a handful, not to hundreds.

The most-diverged fork by a wide margin is \texttt{AyuntamientoMadrid/consul} --- Madrid itself, the city that built Consul. It sits 12{,}725 commits ahead of upstream and 11{,}540 behind, last pushed 2023-05-23: the originating city maintained a private copy that drifted away from the upstream and was eventually abandoned. The architecture pushed even the originator into the fork pattern that fragmented the wider ecosystem.

Figure~\ref{fig:consul-forks} shows when each fork was last pushed. Fork activity peaked in 2019, the year upstream commit activity also collapsed. Only $14\%$ of forks have been pushed in the last three years; the ecosystem ossified rather than grew.

\paragraph{Quality attribute profile.}
\emph{Modifiability} is low: customisation requires forking, and shadow folders reduce file-level conflicts but not logical coupling. Deployability is moderate (standard Rails, but each deployer maintains a separate fork); transparency is moderate too --- the code is open source (AGPL~v3), but the monolithic structure means understanding any feature requires navigating the entire codebase.

\paragraph{Democratic consequence.}
Consul's low modifiability forced a binary choice: accept the platform as-is or fork it entirely. Barcelona forked and rewrote (producing Decidim); Madrid forked its own platform (above); 280 other deployers forked with smaller modifications that mostly never came back upstream. In SECO terms: loss of collective learning, duplicated effort, reduced robustness \citep{jansen2014measuring}. The architecture did not just constrain the code; it constrained who could participate in shaping the platform.


\subsection{Polis: The Contrasting Paradigm}\label{sec:polis}

Polis takes a fundamentally different approach. It does not solve the same problem as Decidim and Consul (structured participation processes). Instead, it synthesizes opinions through algorithmic deliberation. It is included not as a third case in the same comparison, but as evidence of a different trade-off profile.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
Polis has a Clojure backend and a JavaScript frontend. It is monolithic but algorithmically specialised. The core innovation is a pipeline: participants vote agree, disagree, or pass on statements, producing a vote matrix. Principal component analysis (PCA) compresses this matrix into a two-dimensional ``opinion landscape''; k-means clustering then identifies groups of like-minded voters on the landscape. The system finds ``bridging statements'' that resonate across divided groups and ``consensus statements'' agreed on by all clusters. The algorithm is the product; everything else is infrastructure around it.

\paragraph{The different trade-off.}
Polis trades ecosystem breadth for algorithmic depth. There are few third-party modules and a thin contributor ecosystem: 250 forks and 1{,}153 stars on GitHub at our snapshot (2026-04-26), and the commit history reveals an extreme concentration. A single contributor (Mike Bjorkegren) is responsible for $56\%$ of all human commits across the project's 14-year history; the top three authors account for $82\%$ and the top ten for $97\%$. The platform is effectively a one-developer codebase with sporadic external contributions, in contrast to Decidim's 183 authors over a comparable interval. But the tightly coupled architecture ensures the deliberation algorithm works as designed. Docker-based deployment makes it relatively easy to run. Deployers can run it without understanding --- or being able to audit --- the PCA algorithm that determines what counts as consensus.

\paragraph{Quality attribute profile.}
\emph{Modifiability} is low: tight coupling around the core algorithm and no plugin system. \emph{Deployability} is high: Docker containers make spinning up an instance straightforward. \emph{Transparency} is low to moderate: the code is open source, but the algorithmic core --- dimensionality reduction and clustering --- is opaque to most users and deployers. The opinion landscape visualization is readable, but the mechanism producing it is not.

\paragraph{Democratic implication.}
Polis shows that architectural decisions about algorithms are also governance decisions. The PCA + k-means approach embeds an epistemological assumption: that consensus is findable through dimensionality reduction of opinion space. This is not neutral. It determines what kinds of agreement become visible and what kinds remain hidden. \citet{bono2025artificial} identify algorithmic transparency as a key design principle for AI in participation platforms --- a concern that Polis predates but does not fully address. The broader Plurality programme \citep{weyl2025plurality, roy2025conversation} treats algorithmic deliberation as a generative design space rather than a black-box assistant, and positions Polis as one early instance of a larger pattern that combines collective intelligence with statistical mechanism design.


\subsection{Cross-case Synthesis}\label{sec:synthesis}\label{sec:ecosystem-evolution}

The case descriptions in Sections~\ref{sec:decidim}--\ref{sec:polis} surface a consistent pattern: Decidim's modular architecture co-evolved with a shared-module ecosystem, Consul's monolith co-evolved with diverging forks, and Polis's tight coupling co-evolved with algorithmic depth on a thin contributor base. This section anchors that pattern in cross-case empirical evidence (ecosystem-evolution metrics, the three-structure framework, and the trade-offs the cases navigate). The causal direction is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Architecture as Governance in Practice}\label{sec:disc-gov}

The three platforms map onto \citet{iansiti2004strategy}'s ecosystem actor roles. Decidim's technical office operates as a keystone, maintaining shared infrastructure (core engines, manifest pattern, test suite) while enabling third-party modules on top; the 80+ community modules are the niche creators the keystone makes possible. Consul's Madrid team operated as a dominator while the city's institutional backing held: controlling the codebase, refusing the modular fork proposed by Barcelona, offering no mechanism for external contribution. When the institutional backing collapsed in 2019, the dominator withdrew and no successor keystone emerged; the vacuum was filled by 280 modified forks each maintaining its own version in isolation. Polis fits the framework awkwardly --- a single-developer keystone with no surrounding ecosystem to anchor --- and shows the limit case where the architecture cannot support an ecosystem at all.

Decidim's modular architecture did not just make the code flexible --- it made it possible for diverse communities to shape the platform without needing permission. Any organisation can build a module, publish it as a gem, and have it work alongside the official engines. This resembles what \citet{schneider2021modular} call ``modular politics'' --- composable governance primitives implemented as software. The pattern reached its extreme on the Consul side with Madrid itself: the originating city now maintains a fork 12{,}725 commits ahead of upstream, last touched in May 2023 (Section~\ref{sec:consul}). The architecture pushed even the originator into the fragmentation pattern it forced on every other deployer. \citet{schneider2024governable} argues that platform defaults encode governance; Consul's defaults encoded centralisation even though nobody intended it.

Polis shows a different version of the same pattern. PCA + k-means is a decision about what counts as consensus --- not technically wrong, but not neutral either. It makes certain kinds of agreement visible (clusters that converge on bridging statements) and others invisible (minority positions that do not form clean clusters). \citet{bowker1999sorting} call this the politics of classification: every category privileges one view and silences another. PCA decides which axes of disagreement matter; k-means decides where the boundaries between groups fall; opinions that do not align with the principal components are compressed away, still present in the raw data but invisible in the output decision-makers see.

This extends the SECO literature. \citet{christensen2014analysis} describe how software structure shapes organisational and business structures; our cases show that relationship is especially strong in civic tech, where the political consequences of architectural choices are explicit. The two-platform comparison (Section~\ref{sec:consul}) is the clearest example: a shared starting point and political movement, but architectural decisions that co-evolved with very different ecosystems. This parallels \citet{noori2025patterns}'s finding in open-source projects more broadly --- that governance transitions are constrained by early architectural choices. Civic tech is no different, except the stakes are democratic rather than commercial.

\subsection{What SECO Research Gains from Democratic Analysis}\label{sec:disc-seco}

The SECO health dimensions from \citet{jansen2014measuring} --- productivity, robustness, niche creation --- offer useful organising concepts for civic tech, though we apply them as qualitative lenses rather than as full operationalised measurement frameworks. At this coarser level: Decidim's 80+ community modules suggest a productive ecosystem; Consul's fragmentation into diverging forks suggests low robustness; Polis's thin contributor base suggests limited niche creation. But even a fully operationalised health assessment would miss something important: whether the architecture actually enables the democratic participation it claims to support.

A civic tech ecosystem can look ``healthy'' by standard metrics while being architecturally hostile to democratic participation. Consul's 2017--2019 peak generated 14{,}000 commits and 186 unique authors --- numbers a productivity-only health assessment would treat as a thriving project. But the architecture forced adopters into separate forks, and when Ahora Madrid lost the city in 2019 the upstream commit rate dropped $80\%$ overnight (Section~\ref{sec:ecosystem-evolution}). By productivity measures the ecosystem looked fine; by democratic measures it had already failed long before the upstream collapsed.

\paragraph{Fork-divergence shape as a niche-creation diagnostic.}
Standard SECO niche-creation metrics count contributions; they do not distinguish healthy ecosystem extension from silo formation. We propose \emph{fork-divergence shape} --- the population pattern across the ahead-of-upstream and behind-upstream quadrants of a project's forks --- as a niche-creation diagnostic for civic tech platforms. Healthy extension populates the upper-left of the ahead/behind plot: deployers keep up with upstream and add their own work, producing a stream of contributions that the upstream can in principle absorb. Silo formation populates the lower-right and upper-right: forks fall behind upstream while accumulating their own work, producing parallel codebases that drift further apart over time. Figure~\ref{fig:consul-divergence} plots all 1{,}030 enumerable Consul forks. The upper-left quadrant is empty: not a single fork has stayed in sync with upstream while adding its own contributions. The mass sits in the lower-right (unmodified-but-falling-behind) and the upper-right (ahead and behind, the deep silos). This L-shape is the signature of an ecosystem that fragments instead of extending. The shape, not the count, is the diagnostic.

The architecture-as-governance lens adds an evaluative layer beyond shape: not just ``is the ecosystem growing?'' but ``does the architecture enable the participation it claims to support?'' --- what \citet{palacin2024configurations} frame as ``which political values should be enforced by platform design.'' This goes beyond civic tech. Any mission-driven ecosystem --- health technology, education, environmental monitoring --- faces similar tensions. \citet{koskinen2020understanding} show this in DHIS2, the health information platform used in 80+ countries, where data model decisions determine what health authorities can count and report.

\subsection{Implications for the Democracy Stack}\label{sec:disc-stack}

International IDEA \citeyearpar{idea2026democracy} calls for a democracy stack but leaves the architectural decisions implicit --- the report frames the stack in terms of services and rights, not in terms of the technical commitments any such infrastructure must make. \citet{ceps2025building} advocates for interoperable European digital public infrastructure but does not engage the modifiability-against-sustainability trade-off that defines whether interoperable infrastructure can actually be adapted by the communities it is meant to serve. Two lessons from our cases speak directly to these gaps.

\paragraph{Modular over monolithic.}
Trade-off 1 (modifiability against sustainability burden) makes the strongest case for modular architecture. The two-platform comparison in Section~\ref{sec:consul} shows what happens on either side: modularity let independent deployers extend Decidim with 80+ gem-based community modules; the monolith forced Consul deployers to either accept the platform as-is or maintain their own fork, and 280 of them maintain forks today (Section~\ref{sec:disc-gov}). The trade-off is real --- modular maintenance is harder than monolithic maintenance --- but the alternative is fragmentation that the upstream cannot absorb. For a democracy stack that needs to accommodate diverse institutional contexts, the sustainability burden is the price of pluralism. Accept it deliberately.

\paragraph{Algorithmic depth requires a deliberate choice.}
Trade-off 3 (algorithmic depth against ecosystem breadth) is the one a democracy stack cannot dodge by adding more modules. If the democratic problem requires deep algorithms --- consensus discovery in large groups, opinion clustering, statistical bridging between divided constituencies --- accept the thin ecosystem and the centralised expertise that comes with maintaining the algorithm. Polis is an existence proof that the trade is workable when the algorithm is the product. If the democratic problem instead requires broad participation across many institutional contexts, accept simpler computation and modular extension. Decidim is the existence proof on that side. What is not workable is making one a façade for the other: deep algorithms wrapped in module language do not produce broad participation, and modular platforms with one statistical pipeline bolted on do not produce serious algorithmic capability.

\subsection{Evaluating the Approach}\label{sec:evaluation}

Where the approach worked best was the two-platform comparison. The causal chain is unusually clean for a software study: Barcelona proposed modularising Consul, the proposal was rejected, Barcelona rewrote from scratch, and the resulting divergence produced the structural contrast read off in Section~\ref{sec:consul}. But architecture was not the only thing that differed. Barcelona and Madrid had different governance cultures and political trajectories; Madrid's Ahora Madrid government lost power in 2019, hurting Consul's institutional backing. Separating what the architecture caused from what politics caused would need interview research we did not do.

The three tensions captured the most visible trade-offs but missed things. Security is the obvious gap: it did not vary much across our cases, but matters for platforms handling verified identities and votes (Decidim's ElectionGuard integration hints at this). We also stayed at the platform level and said little about hosting, data sovereignty, or vendor dependence.

Lightweight recovery was sufficient to identify trade-offs, but the depth varies. Decidim drew on gemspec parsing, the manifest registry, and logical coupling over the full commit history. Consul added fork-divergence enumeration across all 1{,}030 GitHub forks but no code-level recovery --- the monolithic structure makes that recovery harder and lower-value, but it is a real gap. Polis is thinnest: evolutionary metrics but no module-level recovery, since the architectural unit is the PCA pipeline rather than a set of components. Only Decidim clears the bar of full Symphony-style recovery.

GitHub metrics (stars, forks, contributor counts) are proxies, not direct measures of ecosystem health. Fork counts are particularly ambiguous: our Consul analysis showed that 73\% of forks are unmodified and only 39 of 1{,}030 have 100+ commits ahead. Reporting the aggregate fork count without the breakdown would have overstated the ecosystem's scale. Other commonly cited indicators (stars, deployment counts, contributor totals) carry similar risks.

Four further limitations. \emph{Single coder.} The architectural and ecosystem judgements were made by one author; a second coder could disagree on edge cases (which engine counts as ``shared service'', which fork counts as ``substantively modified''). \emph{Anglophone bias.} Almost all our sources are English; Decidim has substantial Spanish- and Catalan-language documentation, and the Consul fork landscape is heavily Spanish, Catalan, and Latin-American. We likely missed material that would refine our reading. \emph{Decidim-weighted sample.} The Consul and Polis sections are lighter than Decidim's and rely more on cross-case empirical material than independent code-level recovery. \emph{Selection on the dependent variable.} We chose three platforms that exist; the civic tech graveyard --- platforms that never reached production or failed invisibly --- is invisible to this study. The trade-offs we identify are conditional on survival.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper asked what software architecture trade-offs builders of democratic digital infrastructure must navigate. Through multi-case analysis of Decidim, Consul, and Polis, we read three architectural tensions across the cases: modifiability against sustainability burden, interoperability against reduced autonomy, and algorithmic depth against ecosystem breadth. The three platforms navigate them in different directions, with concrete consequences for what democratic communities can do with the resulting infrastructure.

These are not abstract observations. If Copenhagen, or any European city, wanted to build democratic participation infrastructure tomorrow, these tensions are unavoidable. A decade of civic tech experience --- 7{,}000 Decidim commits across 80+ modules, 14{,}000 Consul commits fragmenting into 280 modified forks, 8{,}000 Polis commits dominated by one developer --- shows what each direction looks like in practice.

The architecture-as-governance thesis is well established \citep{winner1980artifacts, lessig2006code, schneider2024governable}; this paper applies it to civic technology using software architecture and SECO methods. For European policymakers, the contribution is that architecture belongs on the same shelf as procurement and budgeting: a governance choice to make deliberately, not a procurement default. For SECO research, it is the fork-divergence shape (Section~\ref{sec:disc-seco}) as a concrete niche-creation diagnostic.

\paragraph{Returning to Denmark.}
Borgerforslag.dk made every trade-off toward simplicity: closed-source, single-purpose, no ecosystem. That works for a petition tool. If Denmark wants participation infrastructure that lets diverse communities deliberate, adapt, and govern, the lessons from a decade of civic tech suggest very different architectural choices. The architecture is the democratic decision.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} discusses limitations in detail. The headline ones: asymmetric case depth (Decidim deep, Consul lighter, Polis lightest); GitHub metrics as proxies; three platforms cannot represent a field with at least 25 architecturally distinct ones; and the quality-attribute-to-democracy mappings are analytical claims, not deployment-validated causal ones.

\paragraph{Future research.}
Two directions invite follow-on work and could each ground a master's thesis. First, stakeholder interviews with Decidim deployers, Consul fork maintainers, and Polis users would complement the repository-mining done here with practitioner evidence and address the causal limitations in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}. Second, a design-science approach: what would a democratic participation architecture for a Nordic municipality actually look like, given the three trade-offs? Such a design exercise would test the principles identified here against real institutional requirements.
