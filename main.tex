% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Europe is investing in democratic digital infrastructure, but there is little guidance on what good software architecture looks like when the goal is democracy rather than service delivery. Architecture shapes governance --- this idea is well established in science and technology studies (STS) and platform studies --- yet to the best of our knowledge, no study has applied software architecture or software ecosystem methods to civic technology platforms. This paper addresses that gap through a multi-case analysis of three platforms: Decidim (deep case with empirical architectural recovery from gemspec files and the commit history), Consul (comparison case --- shared origin, opposite architecture, with fork-divergence analysis across 1{,}030 GitHub forks), and Polis (contrasting algorithmic paradigm). The analysis applies four quality attributes --- modifiability, interoperability, deployability, transparency --- as lenses on the architectural decisions in each case, and shows how each attribute trades off against a corresponding cost (sustainability, autonomy, transparency, ecosystem breadth) that surfaces only when architecture is read as governance. The Decidim/Consul divergence --- same origin, opposite architecture, very different ecosystem outcomes --- provides the strongest evidence that architectural decisions in civic tech function as governance decisions. The fact that Madrid, the city that originally built Consul, ended up maintaining its own fork 12{,}725 commits ahead of upstream, sharpens the point. The paper brings software architecture and ecosystem research into the European policy debate about building a ``democracy stack.''
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Denmark consistently ranks among the most digitized countries in the world, placing first in the EU's Digital Economy and Society Index (DESI) and near the top of the UN e-government rankings.\footnote{European Commission, DESI 2022; UN E-Government Survey 2024.} MitID authenticates millions of citizens. Digital Post has replaced physical mail for government communication. Borger.dk is the portal for every public service. But if a Danish municipality wanted to run a participatory budget or host an online citizen deliberation, there is no platform for it. The only citizen participation tool at the national level is borgerforslag.dk, a closed-source petition site that does exactly one thing: collect signatures. Denmark has built a digital service stack, but not a democracy stack.

Denmark is not alone. Roskilde University's Digital Democracy Index found that ``the foundations for a well-functioning digital democracy are in place --- but the full potential is not being realized'' \citep{roskilde2024democracy}. Across Europe, the gap between digital government services and digital democratic participation keeps growing. This is happening even as the EU invests in democratic digital infrastructure through the Digital Services Act, the European Digital Identity framework, and what International IDEA \citeyearpar{idea2026democracy} calls a ``democracy stack'' --- digital public infrastructure meant to support democratic participation, not just deliver services. But IDEA's report explicitly does not recommend any specific technical architecture; it provides a policy framework without answering what good architecture for democratic infrastructure actually looks like.

At the same time, civic technology platforms have been building democratic infrastructure for over a decade. Decidim, which started in Barcelona, now serves more than 450 institutions in 30 countries. Polis, the algorithmic deliberation tool behind Taiwan's vTaiwan process, has engaged over 200,000 participants and informed 26 pieces of legislation. Consul, originally built for Madrid, has been forked by hundreds of cities and organisations worldwide (we quantify this in Section~\ref{sec:consul}). Together, their experience shows something that the policy conversation tends to miss: software architecture choices directly shape democratic outcomes.

The idea that technical design choices have political consequences is not new. \citet{winner1980artifacts} argued that artifacts have politics. \citet{lessig2006code} showed that code functions as law. \citet{schneider2024governable} demonstrated that online community platforms encode ``implicit feudalism'' through their default governance structures, and \citet{zhang2020policykit} showed that platform software encodes a narrow set of governance possibilities. This insight --- that architecture is governance --- is well established across STS, law, and platform studies.

But to the best of our knowledge, it has not been applied to civic technology using software architecture or software ecosystem methods. Software architecture research offers frameworks for analyzing quality attributes and trade-offs \citep{bass2021software}. SECO research explains how architectural decisions affect ecosystem health and governance \citep{manikas2016revisiting, christensen2014analysis}. Yet neither tradition has examined what ``good architecture'' means when the system is democratic infrastructure, where the quality attributes that matter most are not performance and availability, but modifiability, interoperability, and transparency. \citet{palacin2024configurations} come closest, studying how Decidim's modularity produces 25 distinct technical configurations and asking ``which political values should be enforced by platform design?'' --- but they do not perform architectural analysis. And borgerforslag.dk --- closed-source, single-purpose, centrally built --- shows what happens when democratic software is built without thinking about architecture at all.

This paper asks: \emph{what software architecture trade-offs must builders of democratic digital infrastructure navigate, and what can we learn from the architectural decisions in existing civic tech ecosystems?} We address this through a multi-case architectural decision analysis of three platforms: Decidim (deep case with empirical architectural recovery from the gemspec files and the project's commit history), Consul (comparison case --- shared origin with Decidim but opposite architectural trajectory, with fork-divergence analysis across all enumerable GitHub forks), and Polis (contrasting paradigm --- algorithmic rather than structural approach to participation). We apply four quality attributes as lenses, specified up front from the architecture-as-governance literature --- modifiability, interoperability, deployability, transparency --- and read each architectural decision in each case for the trade-offs it produces between democratic benefit and architectural cost. The result is not a list of trade-offs the cases happened to ``produce''; it is a structured comparison of how each platform navigates the same four tensions in different directions.

The central insight is that architectural decisions in civic tech are governance decisions --- and the Decidim/Consul divergence, where the same starting point led to opposite architectures and radically different ecosystem outcomes, is the clearest demonstration. By applying software architecture and SECO methods to a domain where they have not been used, the paper provides the kind of architectural analysis that IDEA's ``democracy stack'' framework calls for but does not itself deliver.

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Software Architecture for Democratic Infrastructure}\label{sec:sa}

Software architecture is ``the set of structures needed to reason about a system'' --- the elements, the relations between them, and the properties of both \citep[p.~2]{bass2021software}. Architecture decisions shape what a system can and cannot do over its lifetime, mainly through quality attributes: measurable properties like modifiability, interoperability, availability, performance, security, deployability, and testability. In commercial software, these are optimized for business value. When the system is democratic infrastructure, the same reasoning applies but the goal is different: which architecture best supports democratic participation and public legitimacy?

This paper focuses on four quality attributes that are especially important for democratic infrastructure. \emph{Modifiability} is how easily a system can be changed. In democratic infrastructure, it matters because communities with different institutional rules, languages, and participatory traditions need to adapt the platform without hard forking the codebase --- if they cannot, the platform can only accommodate one way of doing things, which limits how many different communities can use it meaningfully. \emph{Interoperability} is how well a system can exchange data and services with others. It determines whether a platform can work with other democratic tools or stays isolated. \emph{Deployability} is how easily new organizations can adopt and run the platform. A platform that requires a lot of technical expertise to deploy will in practice only be available to well-resourced institutions --- so deployability determines who gets to participate. \emph{Transparency} or auditability does not appear in Bass et al.'s standard taxonomy but is essential here. Citizens need to understand what the system does and how it handles their input. A participatory budgeting platform with opaque vote-counting logic undermines the democratic purpose it is supposed to serve.

These attributes pull against each other. Figure~\ref{fig:tradeoffs} positions the three cases on two of them; the other trade-offs are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:tradeoffs}. They are design trade-offs, not problems to solve; \citet{heeks2025three} identify similar tensions at the policy level. The architectural lens makes them concrete: they show up in module boundaries, APIs, and deployment pipelines.

\subsection{Software Ecosystems and Civic Tech}\label{sec:seco}

A software ecosystem (SECO) is ``the interaction of a set of actors on top of a common technological platform, which results in a number of software solutions or services'' \citep{manikas2013systematic}. The concept looks beyond a single product to include the organizations, developers, and users that develop around a shared platform.

\citet{christensen2014analysis} propose that ecosystems can be analyzed through three structures: \emph{organizational} (who is involved and how they relate), \emph{business} (how value and money flow), and \emph{software} (the technical platform and how it can be extended). In commercial ecosystems like app stores, the business structure usually dominates --- Apple's revenue model shapes what apps get built and who builds them. We hypothesize that in civic tech ecosystems, it works differently: the software structure shapes the other two. Technical decisions about modularity and APIs determine which organizations can participate and on what terms. This is not a universal law --- Christensen et al.\ do not claim any fixed ordering between the structures --- but it is a pattern we observe across all three cases and examine in the analysis (see Table~\ref{tab:threestructure}).

\citet{iansiti2004strategy} classify ecosystem actors into roles: keystones create value by sharing assets and enabling others, dominators capture most of the value, and niche creators specialize in narrow segments. These roles map onto civic tech in ways we examine in the analysis: Decidim's technical office operates as a keystone (maintaining shared infrastructure while enabling independent module development), while Consul's Madrid team functioned more as a dominator (controlling the codebase without mechanisms for external contribution). We also draw on ecosystem health dimensions --- productivity, robustness, niche creation \citep{jansen2014measuring} --- as qualitative lenses in the discussion (Section~\ref{sec:disc-seco}).

Ecosystem thinking is clearly useful for civic tech, but the literature has a gap. Manikas's \citeyearpar{manikas2016revisiting} systematic review covered 231 studies on software ecosystems and found none that looked at commons-based, civic, or democratic platforms. Nearly a decade has passed since that review, and we have not found later studies that fill this gap. The ecosystems studied were almost all commercial: app stores, enterprise platforms, proprietary developer ecosystems. \citet{knutas2020local} recognized this gap and proposed applying the OSSECO (open-source software ecosystem) framework to civic tech, but the empirical work was never done. The intersection of SECO analysis and democratic digital infrastructure remains mostly unexplored. This paper begins to fill that gap.

\subsection{Architecture as Governance}\label{sec:arch-gov}

As introduced in Section~\ref{sec:sa}, the idea that architecture functions as governance is well established --- from \citet{winner1980artifacts} through \citet{lessig2006code} to \citet{schneider2024governable}. But this literature has focused mostly on online communities (Reddit, Discord, DAOs) and internet infrastructure, not on civic technology platforms used by public institutions.

The work closest to ours comes from within the Decidim project itself. \citet[p.~68]{barandiaran2024decidim} write that ``Decidim must take into account the democratic implications of any technical decision.'' \citet{palacin2024configurations} studied how Decidim's modularity translates into 25 distinct configurations across 31 participatory budgeting cases, asking ``which political values should be enforced by platform design?'' But neither performs formal software architecture analysis.

Our contribution is to apply architecture-as-governance using software architecture and SECO methods in civic technology --- a combination that has not been tried. We map quality attributes to democratic outcomes: modifiability to pluralism, transparency to legitimacy, interoperability to collaboration, deployability to access. These mappings are reasoned arguments, not proven causal links. Our architectural evidence shows that certain designs \emph{make it possible} for democratic outcomes to happen --- for instance, modularity lets diverse communities adapt the platform --- but proving the outcomes actually materialized would require field studies beyond this paper's scope.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

This study uses a multi-case architectural decision analysis backed by empirical recovery from each platform's source repository. Instead of treating architecture as a static description, we focus on the design decisions that shaped each platform and trace their effects on quality attributes relevant to democratic infrastructure. For each case the method combines four kinds of empirical work: architectural recovery from gemspec and source files, evolutionary analysis from version control history, logical coupling analysis across modules, and fork divergence analysis where it applies. Not every step is run on every case --- Decidim gets the deepest treatment, Consul gets lighter recovery plus fork divergence, and Polis is included as a contrasting paradigm with lighter analysis. We discuss this asymmetry below. Reproducibility scripts and findings logs are kept alongside the paper source.

\paragraph{Case selection.}
The three cases were selected from the wider civic tech landscape using two criteria. First, the platform must have a publicly developed codebase, an active or recently active deployment, and enough documentation to enable architectural recovery --- this rules out closed platforms like borgerforslag.dk, thin demonstrators, and platforms with insufficient documentation. Second, the cases must vary along the architectural dimension we want to study: monolithic versus modular versus algorithmic. Decidim and Consul share an origin in Spain's 15M movement but went in opposite architectural directions, which provides a tight comparison. Polis was added to test whether the same trade-off framework holds for an algorithmically driven platform with a different participation paradigm. Other widely deployed civic tech platforms --- Loomio, CitizenLab, vTaiwan, Adhocracy, LiquidFeedback --- are not analysed here; the three we picked were sufficient to identify the trade-offs without exhausting the field. We return to this scoping in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}.

\paragraph{Architectural recovery.}
For each platform we recover the architecture from primary sources: codebase structure, gemspec and dependency files, extension mechanisms, and official documentation. For Decidim specifically we wrote a script that parses all 27 first-party gemspecs at a pinned snapshot, extracts every \texttt{add\_dependency} call, and classifies each dependency as either an internal Decidim engine or an external Ruby gem. The script also identifies \texttt{Decidim.register\_component} and \texttt{Decidim.register\_participatory\_space} calls in each engine, which is how engines plug into the manifest pattern. The engine dependency graph in Section~\ref{sec:decidim} is recovered from this data, not asserted from documentation. Consul's recovery is lighter and focuses on the points where it contrasts with Decidim --- a single Rails monolith with a file-override extension mechanism, recovered from the codebase structure and documentation. Polis recovery focuses on the data pipeline (vote matrix, PCA, k-means) rather than module structure, since the platform is monolithic and the algorithm is the architectural unit.

\paragraph{Evolutionary analysis.}
For all three platforms we extract commit metadata with \texttt{git log --no-merges} on partial clones (\texttt{git clone --filter=blob:none}). Bot authors (Dependabot, Crowdin, Weblate, GitHub Actions, named release bots) are filtered using email and name patterns. For each repository we compute total human commits, unique human authors, the share of commits attributable to the top three and top ten authors, the Gini coefficient on the per-author commit distribution, and the per-year commit timeline. For Decidim specifically we also extract per-engine commit counts to identify which engines see active development. These metrics serve as ecosystem-health indicators \citep{manikas2013systematic, jansen2014measuring} and underpin the comparative analysis in Section~\ref{sec:analysis}.

\paragraph{Logical coupling.}
Following \citet{gall1998detection}, we measure which Decidim engines change together over the project history. For each non-merge, non-bot commit we identify which top-level engine directories the commit touches, count co-occurrences across all engine pairs, and compute the Jaccard similarity --- commits touching both engines divided by commits touching either --- as the coupling metric. Mega-commits touching more than eight engines are excluded as refactor or release commits that would otherwise dominate the signal. This analysis tests whether the manifest pattern produces architectural isolation at the change level, not only at the gemspec level.

\paragraph{Fork divergence.}
For Consul we use the GitHub REST API to enumerate forks of \texttt{consuldemocracy/consuldemocracy} (formerly \texttt{consul/consul}; the project was renamed) and call the compare endpoint for each, returning the number of commits each fork is ahead of and behind upstream master. The result classifies each fork along two axes: \emph{modified} (at least one commit ahead) versus \emph{unmodified}, and \emph{active} (pushed in the last two years) versus \emph{dormant}. This quantifies the paper's earlier claim that deployers maintain isolated forks with little upstream contribution. Polis and Decidim are not subject to fork-divergence analysis: Decidim deployers use the gem-plugin mechanism rather than forks, and Polis has too small a fork count and a different deployment model.

\paragraph{Quality attribute scope.}
The four quality attributes --- modifiability, interoperability, deployability, transparency --- were specified up front, not induced from the cases. We chose them because they are where civic tech platforms most clearly diverge from commercial systems and where the architecture-as-governance literature predicts the strongest political consequences. \citet{bass2021software} list many more quality attributes. Security and availability matter for any system, but the architectural divergence between our three cases is most visible in the four we chose. This is a scoping choice, not a claim that other attributes do not matter. For each case we identify the architectural decisions that visibly trade off between these attributes --- modular versus monolithic organisation, plugin versus fork extension, shared versus isolated data --- drawing on the architectural decision record tradition \citep{tyree2005architecture} as a way of structuring the analysis without producing formal ADRs.

\paragraph{Asymmetric case design.}
The three cases are intentionally treated with different depth. Decidim is the deep case: full architectural recovery, per-engine evolutionary analysis, and logical coupling. Its modular monolith --- a Ruby on Rails monorepo of first-party engines organised through a manifest pattern --- offers the most architecturally rich example available in civic tech. Consul is the comparison case: lighter recovery focused on the contrasts with Decidim, evolutionary analysis, and fork divergence. The two platforms share an origin in Spain's 15M movement and briefly shared early code, but went in opposite architectural directions, which approximates --- though does not constitute --- a natural experiment. The architectural divergence was a deliberate choice shaped by organisational and political factors as well \citep{barandiaran2024decidim}, which we revisit in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}. Polis is a contrasting paradigm rather than a third equal case: algorithmic deliberation through PCA and k-means clustering rather than structured participation. The Polis treatment is shorter --- no fork-divergence analysis, no per-module recovery --- and serves to test whether the trade-off framework extends beyond the participatory-platform pattern.

\paragraph{Data sources and limitations.}
Primary sources are technical: the public GitHub repositories of all three platforms at pinned snapshots, gemspec and dependency files for Decidim, application structure for Consul, runtime components for Polis. Secondary sources include \citet{barandiaran2024decidim} on Decidim's sociotechnical design, the OSOR case study on Consul's European deployments \citep{linaker2025consul}, the NTARI fork report \citep{ntari2025consul}, and the Codegram architecture blog on early Decidim design decisions \citep{codegram-decidim}. The most important limitation, discussed further in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}, is that our quality-attribute-to-democracy mappings are analytical arguments, not empirically validated causal claims. Architectural recovery captures dependency structure but not behaviour at runtime. The evolutionary metrics use email-based author identity, which conflates authors who used multiple emails over time; the directional findings are robust to this but absolute numbers are approximate. Fork analysis covers public forks only; private forks are invisible to the GitHub API.

\section{Analysis}\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Decidim: Architecture Recovery}\label{sec:decidim}

Decidim is a modular monolith built with Ruby on Rails. The monorepo at \url{github.com/decidim/decidim} contains 27 first-party engines packaged as standalone Ruby gems --- 24 user-facing platform engines and three developer-tooling engines (\texttt{design}, \texttt{dev}, \texttt{generators}). This section recovers the architecture from the codebase and documentation, focusing on the decisions that shaped the platform's trajectory. Recovery is pinned to commit \texttt{be39c244} (2026-05-12).

\paragraph{Engine structure.}
The 24 user-facing engines fall into four groups. \texttt{decidim-core} is the foundational dependency. Three infrastructure engines sit above it: \texttt{admin}, \texttt{api}, and \texttt{system}. Three shared-service engines act as platform-wide services that other engines depend on: \texttt{comments}, \texttt{forms}, and \texttt{verifications}. Four participatory spaces are containers for participation: \texttt{processes}, \texttt{assemblies}, \texttt{conferences}, and \texttt{initiatives}. The remaining 13 engines are components that attach to spaces: \texttt{proposals}, \texttt{meetings}, \texttt{budgets}, \texttt{debates}, \texttt{accountability}, \texttt{surveys}, \texttt{blogs}, \texttt{pages}, \texttt{elections}, \texttt{templates}, \texttt{collaborative\_texts}, \texttt{demographics}, and \texttt{ai}. The \texttt{ai}, \texttt{demographics}, and \texttt{collaborative\_texts} engines are recent additions visible in the commit history; \texttt{decidim-sortitions} existed in earlier versions but was removed on 2025-11-19. All engines are prefixed \texttt{decidim-} and packaged as separate gems.

\paragraph{Dependency graph.}
Every engine except \texttt{decidim-core} itself declares \texttt{add\_dependency "decidim-core"} in its gemspec. So far the architecture matches the textbook description of a hub-and-spoke pattern. But the graph is denser than that --- 13 of the 27 engines also have runtime dependencies on other Decidim engines besides core. The most-depended-on engines are \texttt{decidim-comments} (6 dependents: \texttt{accountability}, \texttt{blogs}, \texttt{budgets}, \texttt{debates}, \texttt{initiatives}, \texttt{proposals}) and \texttt{decidim-forms} (5 dependents: \texttt{demographics}, \texttt{elections}, \texttt{meetings}, \texttt{surveys}, \texttt{templates}). These two engines are de-facto Layer-2 hubs of the platform rather than peers of the infrastructure engines, even though they are categorised as ``shared services'' alongside \texttt{verifications}. One cross-engine dependency is architecturally notable: \texttt{decidim-conferences} (a participatory space) runtime-depends on \texttt{decidim-meetings} (a component), which means the conferences space hard-requires meetings rather than treating it as an optional plugin. We come back to this below. Figure~\ref{fig:dependency} shows the full graph, recovered from the gemspec files.

\paragraph{The space/component matrix.}
This is the key architectural pattern. \citet[p.~82]{barandiaran2024decidim} use the metaphor that ``participatory components are to participatory spaces what furniture is to rooms.'' An administrator can mix and match features (proposals, budgets, surveys, debates) with participation contexts (processes, assemblies, conferences) without writing any code. Want proposals inside a participatory process? Turn it on. Want budgets inside an assembly? Turn it on. Almost any component can attach to almost any space, creating an $N \times M$ combinatorial matrix \citep[p.~75]{barandiaran2024decidim}. Technically, this works through a shared database table in \texttt{decidim-core} that stores which component is attached to which space, using a flexible association pattern. Figure~\ref{fig:matrix} illustrates this composition. The matrix is not entirely free, though: as noted above, \texttt{decidim-conferences} has a runtime dependency on \texttt{decidim-meetings} at the gem level, so the meetings component is effectively required by the conferences space rather than optional. The pattern is mostly clean, with at least this one exception.

\paragraph{Four interaction mechanisms.}
The engines interact through four mechanisms that define the internal API boundaries:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \emph{Rails engine mounting.} Each engine uses \texttt{isolate\_namespace} to keep its code separate. Component engines are mounted dynamically under the space's URL scope at runtime.
  \item \emph{Manifest and registry pattern.} \texttt{decidim-core} maintains global registries for components and spaces (\texttt{ComponentManifest}, \texttt{ParticipatorySpaceManifest}). Engines register themselves at boot time by calling \texttt{Decidim.register\_component} or \texttt{Decidim.register\_participatory\_space}. Core reads from these registries to build menus, set up routes, and expose the API.
  \item \emph{Shared database.} All engines write to a single PostgreSQL database. Cross-engine data access happens through ActiveRecord associations, not service calls. This means tight data coupling, but it avoids the overhead of a distributed system.
  \item \emph{Pub/sub events.} \texttt{Decidim::EventsManager.publish} wraps \texttt{ActiveSupport::Notifications}, letting modules trigger cross-engine side effects (e.g., notifying followers when a proposal is published) without direct dependencies.
\end{enumerate}

Listing~\ref{lst:register} shows the manifest/registry pattern concretely. This is the interface that both first-party and third-party modules use to register themselves with the platform.

\paragraph{Module isolation in practice.}
The gemspec dependencies and the manifest pattern describe what the engines \emph{can} do, not what they actually do over time. To check whether the platform's modularity is matched by modular evolution, we apply Gall et al.'s logical coupling analysis \citep{gall1998detection} to the project's commit history: for each non-merge, non-bot commit, we identify which engines it touches, count co-occurrences, and compute the Jaccard similarity for every engine pair (commits touching both / commits touching either). Of 7{,}078 human commits, $64\%$ stay inside a single engine boundary --- the architecture provides real, not nominal, isolation. The cross-engine coupling that does exist is heavily clustered, shown as the heatmap in Figure~\ref{fig:coupling}. Three patterns stand out. First, the four participatory spaces (\texttt{processes}, \texttt{assemblies}, \texttt{conferences}, \texttt{initiatives}) co-change at Jaccard up to $0.43$ despite having no gemspec dependencies between them --- the ``space'' abstraction is unified at the source level rather than implemented as four independent realisations of a clean interface. Second, the flagship components (\texttt{proposals}, \texttt{meetings}, \texttt{budgets}, \texttt{debates}) co-evolve at Jaccard $0.11$--$0.20$ because real deployments use them together and share UI patterns. Third, and counter-intuitively, engines that gemspec-depend on \texttt{decidim-comments} or \texttt{decidim-forms} barely co-change with them ($J < 0.06$ in most cases): the shared services have stable APIs, and the manifest pattern works as advertised for API consumption. The modularity is at API boundaries, not at implementation.

\paragraph{Third-party extension mechanism.}
Community modules use the same interface as first-party ones. A developer creates a Ruby gem, declares a dependency on \texttt{decidim-core}, and calls \texttt{Decidim.register\_component} in an initializer. The module is installed by adding one line to the deployer's \texttt{Gemfile}. It is auto-discovered through Rails engine loading. A CLI scaffold (\texttt{decidim --component my-component}) generates boilerplate. This design has produced over 80 community-developed modules (listed in the Decidim module directory and published as Ruby gems) that work alongside the core engines.

\paragraph{Quality attribute profile.}
\emph{Modifiability} is high. The engine architecture lets deployers add, remove, or replace components without touching core code. Customization happens through modules, not forks. \emph{Interoperability} is moderate. There is a GraphQL API for data access, and integration choices are deliberately open-source (OpenStreetMap instead of Google Maps, Matomo instead of Google Analytics, Jitsi instead of Zoom). But there are no cross-platform standards for civic tech interoperability. \emph{Deployability} is moderate to low. Setup is complex: a Rails application, module configuration, database, and the full set of engines that need to work together. Multi-tenancy helps --- one installation can serve multiple organizations --- but the initial barrier is still high. \emph{Transparency} is high. Proposals are sorted randomly to prevent a ``rich get richer'' dynamic. Administrators cannot delete proposals, which serves as an anti-censorship measure. The project frames its design as ``participant experience (PX)'' rather than ``user experience (UX).'' All code is licensed under AGPL~v3.

\paragraph{Ecosystem indicators.}
As of May 2026, \texttt{decidim/decidim} has 1{,}748 stars and 469 forks on GitHub. The platform is deployed in over 450 institutions across 30 countries; Barcelona alone has over 120{,}000 registered participants. The technical office consists of only three employees, and the project is critically dependent on Catalan institutional funding \citep{barandiaran2024decidim}. Mining the commit history (Section~\ref{sec:method}) gives a sharper picture of ecosystem health: 7{,}078 human commits across 183 unique human authors since 2016, with the top three contributors accounting for $36\%$ of commits and the top ten for $68\%$ (Gini coefficient on the per-author distribution: $0.85$). \citet{cobos2025comparison} characterise this as activity that ``tends to concentrate on a few contributors''; the framing is directionally right, though Section~\ref{sec:synthesis} shows that Decidim is actually the most distributed of the three cases in this study. All 24 user-facing engines see commits in the last 12 months, including three recently added engines (\texttt{ai}, \texttt{demographics}, \texttt{collaborative\_texts}). The ecosystem is productive and diverse but its robustness is fragile.


\subsection{Consul: The Comparison Case}\label{sec:consul}

Consul and Decidim share an origin. Both emerged from Spain's 15M/Indignados movement. Barcelona initially contributed to Consul (which powered decide.madrid.es), but proposed modularizing the codebase. According to the Codegram architecture blog, ``the talks on this document didn't come to a successful end'' \citep{codegram-decidim}. Barcelona then rewrote from scratch, creating Decidim in 2016--2017. The architectural divergence between the two platforms starts here. The project's GitHub home has since been renamed from \texttt{consul/consul} to \texttt{consuldemocracy/consuldemocracy}; both URLs redirect to the new location.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
Consul is a single monolithic Rails application. There is no gem separation and no engine isolation. Features --- proposals, debates, budgets, legislation, voting, polls --- all share the same codebase, the same \texttt{ApplicationController}, and the same routes file. The \texttt{config/routes.rb} file defines all feature routes in a single file. Models live in \texttt{app/models/} with functional subdirectories (for example, \texttt{budget/investment.rb}), but there are no module-level namespace boundaries enforced at the code level --- a budget model can freely reference a proposal model without going through any API or registry. All tables sit in one database schema with no migration isolation. There is no \texttt{ARCHITECTURE.md} and no \texttt{Gemfile} separation of features into independent packages. Technical documentation covers installation and customization, but does not describe the architecture formally.

\paragraph{Extension mechanism.}
Consul uses what can be called a ``custom shadow folder'' pattern. The documentation describes a file-override system: to change how a budget investment works, you create a file at the same path under \texttt{app/models/custom/}, which overrides the original. This is monkey-patching, not module isolation. The practical effect is that customization requires forking the entire repository --- the documentation's ``Getting Started'' step~1 is literally ``Create your fork.'' The deployer then maintains that fork across upgrades manually, resolving conflicts between their custom overrides and upstream changes every release. A \texttt{Gemfile\_custom} convention exists for adding dependencies, but these load into the same process, not as separately versioned components.

\paragraph{The fork problem.}
The OSOR/Interoperable Europe case study found ``fragmented development, with multiple forks adapted to different local needs\ldots\ difficult to synchronise updates and maintain security across installations'' \citep{linaker2025consul}. It also reports that ``some expressed the belief that Consul felt like it was being forked by each local government\ldots\ with little getting contributed back upstream.'' To make this concrete, we enumerated all forks of \texttt{consuldemocracy/consuldemocracy} via the GitHub API (Section~\ref{sec:method}) and computed the divergence of each against upstream master. The repository has 1{,}123 reported forks; the API returns 1{,}030 enumerable records (the gap is private and deleted forks). Of those, 746 ($73\%$) are unmodified --- ``stars in fork form'', created but never edited. 280 ($27\%$) have at least one commit ahead of upstream; this number is what previous reports approximate when they say ``around 250 deployments'' \citep{ntari2025consul}. Within those 280, the distribution is heavily skewed: 147 forks (53\%) have fewer than ten commits ahead; only 39 have 100 or more, and only 5 have 1{,}000 or more. The ``deployer maintains a fully separate codebase'' picture applies to a handful, not to hundreds.

The most-diverged fork by a wide margin is \texttt{AyuntamientoMadrid/consul} --- Madrid itself, the city that originally built Consul. It sits 12{,}725 commits ahead of upstream and 11{,}540 commits behind, last pushed 2023-05-23. The city that originated the platform ended up maintaining a private copy that drifted away from the public upstream and was eventually abandoned --- the architecture pushed even the originator into the fork pattern that fragmented the wider ecosystem.

Figure~\ref{fig:consul-forks} shows when each fork was last pushed. Fork-creation peaked in 2019, the same year upstream commit activity collapsed (Section~\ref{sec:method} describes the timeline). Only $14\%$ of forks have been pushed in the last three years; the rest are stale. The ecosystem did not slowly grow and consolidate --- it ossified.

\paragraph{Democratic consequence.}
Consul's low modifiability forced a binary choice: accept the platform as-is, or fork it entirely. Barcelona chose to fork and rewrite, producing Decidim. Madrid --- the city that built Consul in the first place --- chose to fork its own platform: \texttt{AyuntamientoMadrid/consul} sits 12{,}725 commits ahead of upstream and was abandoned in 2023. Some 280 other deployers forked with smaller modifications that mostly never came back upstream. In SECO terms, this means loss of collective learning, duplicated effort, and reduced robustness \citep{jansen2014measuring}. The architecture did not just constrain the code --- it constrained who could participate in shaping the platform, and pushed even the originator into the fragmentation pattern.


\subsection{Polis: The Contrasting Paradigm}\label{sec:polis}

Polis takes a fundamentally different approach. It does not solve the same problem as Decidim and Consul (structured participation processes). Instead, it synthesizes opinions through algorithmic deliberation. It is included not as a third case in the same comparison, but as evidence of a different trade-off profile.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
Polis has a Clojure backend and a JavaScript frontend. It is monolithic but algorithmically specialized. The core innovation is a pipeline: participants vote agree, disagree, or pass on statements, producing a vote matrix. Principal component analysis (PCA) --- a statistical technique that reduces many variables to a few key dimensions --- compresses this matrix into a two-dimensional ``opinion landscape'' that can be visualized. K-means clustering (a standard machine learning algorithm that groups similar data points, with $k$ selected automatically) then identifies groups of like-minded voters on this landscape. The system then finds ``bridging statements'' that resonate across divided groups and ``consensus statements'' agreed on by all clusters. This algorithm is the product --- everything else is infrastructure around it.

\paragraph{The different trade-off.}
Polis trades ecosystem breadth for algorithmic depth. There are few third-party modules and a thin contributor ecosystem: 250 forks and 1{,}153 stars on GitHub at our snapshot (2026-04-26), and the commit history reveals an extreme concentration. A single contributor (Mike Bjorkegren) is responsible for $56\%$ of all human commits across the project's 14-year history; the top three authors account for $82\%$ and the top ten for $97\%$. The platform is effectively a one-developer codebase with sporadic external contributions, in contrast to Decidim's 183 authors over a comparable interval. But the tightly coupled architecture ensures the deliberation algorithm works as designed. Docker-based deployment makes it relatively easy to run. Deployers can run it without understanding --- or being able to audit --- the PCA algorithm that determines what counts as consensus.

\paragraph{Quality attribute profile.}
\emph{Modifiability} is low: tight coupling around the core algorithm and no plugin system. \emph{Deployability} is high: Docker containers make spinning up an instance straightforward. \emph{Transparency} is low to moderate: the code is open source, but the algorithmic core --- dimensionality reduction and clustering --- is opaque to most users and deployers. The opinion landscape visualization is readable, but the mechanism producing it is not.

\paragraph{Democratic implication.}
Polis shows that architectural decisions about algorithms are also governance decisions. The PCA + k-means approach embeds an epistemological assumption: that consensus is findable through dimensionality reduction of opinion space. This is not neutral. It determines what kinds of agreement become visible and what kinds remain hidden. \citet{bono2025artificial} identify algorithmic transparency as a key design principle for AI in participation platforms --- a concern that Polis predates but does not fully address.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Architecture as Governance in Practice}\label{sec:disc-gov}

Decidim's modular architecture did not just make the code flexible --- it made it possible for diverse communities to shape the platform without needing permission from the core team. Any organization can build a module, publish it as a gem, and have it work alongside the official engines. This resembles what \citet{schneider2021modular} call ``modular politics'' --- composable governance primitives implemented as software. Decidim achieves something analogous at the participation-design level, where spaces and components can be combined freely (Section~\ref{sec:decidim}). Consul's monolith, by contrast, concentrated decision-making. If you wanted to change how the platform works, you either convinced the Madrid team or you forked. In practice most deployers forked, and the changes stayed local. The pattern reached its extreme with Madrid itself: the originating city now maintains a fork that is 12{,}725 commits ahead of upstream and was last touched in May 2023 (Section~\ref{sec:consul}). The architecture pushed even the originator into the fragmentation pattern that it forced on every other deployer. \citet{schneider2024governable} argues that platform defaults encode governance --- Consul's defaults encoded centralization, even though nobody intended it.

Polis shows a different version of the same pattern. The PCA + k-means algorithm is a decision about what counts as consensus. It is not technically wrong --- it is a reasonable approach to finding common ground in large groups. But it is also not neutral. It makes certain kinds of agreement visible (clusters that converge on bridging statements) and other kinds invisible (minority positions that do not form clean clusters). As \citet{bowker1999sorting} argue, classification systems are political because every category privileges one view and silences another. Dimensionality reduction on opinion space is a classification system: PCA decides which axes of disagreement matter most, and k-means decides where the boundaries between groups fall. Opinions that do not align with the principal components get compressed away --- they are still there in the raw data, but invisible in the output that decision-makers see.

This extends the SECO literature into new territory. \citet{christensen2014analysis} describe how software structure shapes organizational and business structures. Our cases show that this relationship is especially strong in civic tech, where the political consequences of architectural choices are explicit. The Decidim/Consul divergence is the clearest example: the same starting point, the same political movement, but opposite architectural decisions produced radically different ecosystems. This parallels what \citet{noori2025patterns} find in open-source projects more broadly --- that governance transitions are constrained by early architectural choices. Civic tech is no different, except that the stakes are democratic rather than commercial.

\subsection{What SECO Research Gains from Democratic Analysis}\label{sec:disc-seco}

The SECO health dimensions from \citet{jansen2014measuring} --- productivity, robustness, niche creation --- offer useful organizing concepts for civic tech, though we apply them as qualitative lenses rather than with their full operationalized measurement frameworks (which would require longitudinal data on application survival rates, revenue flows, and developer retention that we do not have). At this coarser level: Decidim's 80+ community modules suggest a productive ecosystem; Consul's fragmentation into diverging forks suggests low robustness (the ecosystem cannot easily absorb disruptions or share improvements); Polis's thin contributor base suggests limited niche creation. But even a fully operationalized health assessment would miss something important: it would not tell us whether the architecture actually enables the democratic participation it claims to support.

A civic tech ecosystem can look ``healthy'' by standard metrics while being architecturally hostile to democratic participation. Consul's peak in 2017--2019 generated 14{,}000 commits and 186 unique authors --- the kind of numbers that a productivity-only health assessment would treat as a thriving project. But the architecture forced adopters into separate forks, and when Ahora Madrid lost the city in 2019 the upstream commit rate dropped $80\%$ overnight (Section~\ref{sec:ecosystem-evolution}). By productivity measures the ecosystem looked fine; by democratic measures --- whether diverse communities can meaningfully shape the platform --- it had already failed long before the upstream collapsed.

The architecture-as-governance lens adds an evaluative layer: not just ``is the ecosystem growing?'' but ``does the architecture enable the participation it claims to support?'' --- what \citet{palacin2024configurations} frame as ``which political values should be enforced by platform design.'' This goes beyond civic tech. Any mission-driven ecosystem --- health technology, education, environmental monitoring --- faces similar tensions. \citet{koskinen2020understanding} show this in DHIS2, the health information platform used in 80+ countries, where data model decisions determine what health authorities can count and report.

\subsection{Implications for the Democracy Stack}\label{sec:disc-stack}

International IDEA \citeyearpar{idea2026democracy} proposes building a ``democracy stack'' --- digital public infrastructure designed to support democratic rights and participation. \citet{ceps2025building} advocates for interoperable European digital public infrastructure. But neither specifies what good architecture looks like in this context.

Our cases point to some concrete lessons. The Decidim/Consul comparison makes the strongest case for modular over monolithic architecture --- modularity let communities adapt the platform, while the monolith forced forking and fragmentation. Interoperability standards should be open, but standards that ignore local variation risk excluding the communities they are meant to serve. Deployability matters for access, though not at the cost of transparency --- Polis is easy to deploy but hard to audit. And the governance structure should match how power is distributed in the architecture. Decidim's community governance (the Decidim Association, Metadecidim) works partly because it mirrors the distributed technical design.

\subsection{Evaluating the Approach}\label{sec:evaluation}

The approach worked well in some places and less well in others.

Where it worked best was the Decidim/Consul divergence. The causal chain there is unusually clean for a software study: Barcelona proposed modularizing Consul, the proposal was rejected, Barcelona rewrote from scratch, and ten years later the two platforms have opposite architectures and very different ecosystems. That said, architecture was not the only thing that differed. Barcelona and Madrid also had different governance cultures and different political trajectories --- Madrid's Ahora Madrid government lost power in 2019, which hurt Consul's institutional backing. Separating what the architecture caused from what politics caused would need interview-based research we did not do.

The four quality attributes captured the most visible trade-offs, but probably missed things. Security is the obvious gap --- it did not vary much across our three cases, but for platforms handling verified identities and votes it clearly matters. Decidim's ElectionGuard integration for end-to-end verifiable elections hints at this. We also stayed at the platform level and said little about hosting, data sovereignty, or vendor dependence, which may matter just as much for democratic outcomes.

Lightweight recovery was sufficient to identify key decisions and trade-offs, but the depth of analysis varies across cases. The Decidim analysis drew on gemspec parsing, the manifest registry, and logical coupling over the full commit history. The Consul analysis added a fork-divergence enumeration across all 1{,}030 enumerable GitHub forks but did not perform the same code-level recovery as Decidim --- the monolithic structure makes that recovery harder and lower-value, but it is a real gap and a fair criticism. The Polis analysis remains the thinnest: evolutionary metrics from the commit history but no module-level recovery, since the platform is monolithic and the architectural unit is the PCA pipeline rather than a set of components. The asymmetric depth is intentional but should not be papered over: only the Decidim case clears the bar of full architectural recovery in the sense Symphony \citep{tyree2005architecture} envisages.

Finally, GitHub metrics --- stars, forks, contributor counts --- are proxies, not direct measures of ecosystem health. Fork counts are particularly ambiguous: our Consul analysis (Section~\ref{sec:consul}) showed that 73\% of forks are unmodified and only 39 of 1{,}030 have 100+ commits ahead of upstream. The aggregate fork count was a poor proxy for active deployments before we computed the divergence; reporting it without the breakdown would have overstated the ecosystem's scale. Other commonly cited indicators --- stars, deployment counts, contributor totals --- carry similar risks of misinterpretation.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper asked what software architecture trade-offs builders of democratic digital infrastructure must navigate. Through multi-case analysis of Decidim, Consul, and Polis, we applied four quality attributes from the architecture-as-governance literature --- modifiability, interoperability, deployability, transparency --- as lenses on the architectural decisions in each case. Each attribute trades off against a corresponding architectural cost: sustainability burden, reduced autonomy, lost transparency, ecosystem thinness. Reading those trade-offs across the three platforms shows the same four tensions navigated in different directions, with concrete consequences for what democratic communities can do with the resulting platforms.

These are not abstract observations. If Copenhagen, or any European city, wanted to build democratic participation infrastructure tomorrow, these are the tensions they would face. A decade of civic tech experience --- 7{,}000 commits of Decidim modules, 14{,}000 commits of Consul fragmenting into 280 modified forks, 8{,}000 commits of Polis dominated by one developer --- shows what happens when you navigate them in one direction versus another.

The architecture-as-governance thesis is well established in STS, law, and platform studies \citep{winner1980artifacts, lessig2006code, schneider2024governable}. This paper shows what it looks like in practice when applied to civic technology through software architecture and SECO methods. The Decidim/Consul divergence --- same origin, opposite architecture, radically different ecosystem outcomes --- is the clearest demonstration. For European policymakers debating democratic digital public infrastructure, this means that architecture is not a procurement detail --- it is a governance choice. And for SECO research, it means that ecosystem health metrics need a normative layer: not just ``is the ecosystem growing?'' but ``does the architecture actually support the democratic values it claims to serve?''

\paragraph{Returning to Denmark.}
Borgerforslag.dk made every trade-off in the direction of simplicity: closed-source, single-purpose, no ecosystem. That works for a petition tool. But if Denmark wants participation infrastructure that lets diverse communities deliberate, adapt, and govern, the architectural lessons from a decade of civic tech suggest very different choices. The architecture is the democratic decision.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The recovery is lightweight rather than formal: Decidim has the deepest analysis (gemspec parsing, logical coupling over the full commit history); Consul is recovered at the structural level and quantified through fork-divergence analysis, but not at the same code-level depth; Polis sits on evolutionary metrics plus published descriptions. GitHub metrics are proxies, not direct measures of democratic impact. Three platforms, however carefully selected, cannot represent all civic tech architectures --- the field includes at least 25 platforms with genuine architectural variety (from Django-based Adhocracy to LiquidFeedback's database-as-application approach). The mappings from quality attributes to democratic outcomes are analytical claims, not empirically validated through deployment studies.

\paragraph{Future research.}
Two directions are promising, and both could serve as the basis for a master's thesis building on this project. First, stakeholder interviews with Decidim deployers, Consul fork maintainers, and Polis users would ground the architecture-governance connection in direct evidence from practitioners, complementing the repository-mining and architectural analysis carried out here. This would address the causal limitations discussed in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}. Second, a design-oriented approach: what would a democratic participation architecture for a Nordic municipality actually look like, given these trade-offs? A design science approach could test the principles identified here against real institutional requirements.
