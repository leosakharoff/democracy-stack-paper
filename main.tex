% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Europe is investing in democratic digital infrastructure, but there is little guidance on what good software architecture looks like when the goal is democracy rather than service delivery. Architecture shapes governance --- this idea is well established in STS and platform research --- yet no study has applied software architecture or software ecosystem methods to civic technology platforms. This paper addresses that gap through a multi-case analysis of three platforms: Decidim (deep case with architectural recovery), Consul (comparison case --- shared origin, opposite architecture), and Polis (contrasting algorithmic paradigm). The analysis identifies four quality attribute trade-offs that are both technical and political: modifiability versus sustainability, interoperability versus autonomy, deployability versus transparency, and algorithmic sophistication versus ecosystem breadth. The Decidim/Consul divergence --- same origin, opposite architecture, very different ecosystem outcomes --- provides the strongest evidence that architectural decisions in civic tech function as governance decisions. The paper connects software architecture and ecosystem research to the emerging European conversation about a ``democracy stack.''
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Denmark is one of the most digitized countries in the world. MitID authenticates over six million citizens. Digital Post has replaced physical mail for government communication. Borger.dk is the portal for every public service. Denmark consistently tops the UN e-government rankings. But if a Danish municipality wanted to run a participatory budget or host an online citizen deliberation, there is no platform for it. There is no ecosystem and no architecture. The only citizen participation tool at the national level is borgerforslag.dk, which was custom-built by the private agency Charlie Tango. It is closed-source and does exactly one thing: collect signatures for parliamentary petitions. Denmark has built a digital service stack, but not a democracy stack.

Denmark is not alone. Roskilde University's Digital Democracy Index found that ``the foundations for a well-functioning digital democracy are in place --- but the full potential is not being realized'' \citep{roskilde2024democracy}. Across Europe, the gap between digital government services and digital democratic participation keeps growing. This is happening even as the EU invests in democratic digital infrastructure through the Digital Services Act, the European Digital Identity framework, and what International IDEA \citeyearpar{idea2026democracy} calls a ``democracy stack'' --- digital public infrastructure meant to support democratic participation, not just deliver services.

At the same time, civic technology platforms have been building democratic infrastructure for over a decade. Decidim, which started in Barcelona, now serves more than 450 institutions in 30 countries. Polis, the algorithmic deliberation tool behind Taiwan's vTaiwan process, has engaged over 200,000 participants and informed 26 pieces of legislation. Consul, originally built for Madrid, has been deployed in over 250 cities. Together, their experience shows something that the policy conversation tends to miss: software architecture choices directly shape democratic outcomes.

The idea that technical design choices have political consequences is not new. \citet{winner1980artifacts} argued that artifacts have politics. \citet{lessig2006code} showed that code functions as law --- regulating behavior through architecture as effectively as through legislation. More recently, \citet{schneider2024governable} has demonstrated that online community platforms encode ``implicit feudalism'' through their default governance structures, and \citet{zhang2020policykit} showed that ``the software behind online community platforms encodes a governance model that represents a strikingly narrow set of governance possibilities.'' This insight --- that architecture is governance --- is now well established across science and technology studies, law, and platform studies.

But it has not been applied to civic technology using software architecture or software ecosystem methods. Software architecture research offers frameworks for analyzing quality attributes and trade-offs \citep{bass2021software}. SECO research explains how architectural decisions affect ecosystem health and governance \citep{manikas2016revisiting, christensen2014analysis}. Yet neither tradition has examined what ``good architecture'' means when the system is democratic infrastructure --- where the quality attributes that matter most are not performance and availability, but modifiability (can different communities adapt the platform without forking?), interoperability (can platforms work together?), and transparency (can citizens understand what the system does?). \citet{palacin2024configurations} come closest, studying how Decidim's modularity produces 25 distinct technical configurations and asking ``which political values should be enforced by platform design?'' --- but they do not perform architectural analysis. And borgerforslag.dk --- closed-source, single-purpose, centrally built --- shows what happens when democratic software is built without thinking about architecture at all.

This paper asks: \emph{what software architecture trade-offs must builders of democratic digital infrastructure navigate, and what can we learn from the architectural decisions in existing civic tech ecosystems?} We address this through a multi-case architectural decision analysis of three platforms: Decidim (deep case with full architectural recovery), Consul (comparison case --- shared origin with Decidim but opposite architectural trajectory), and Polis (contrasting paradigm --- algorithmic rather than structural approach to participation). The analysis identifies four quality attribute trade-offs that are both technical and political: modifiability versus sustainability, interoperability versus autonomy, deployability versus transparency, and algorithmic sophistication versus ecosystem breadth.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it applies software architecture and SECO methods to civic technology --- a domain where architectural analysis is largely absent despite a decade of real-world evidence. Second, it shows how architecture-as-governance plays out in practice through the Decidim/Consul divergence, where the same starting point led to opposite architectural choices and very different ecosystem outcomes. Third, it connects these findings to the emerging European conversation about democratic digital public infrastructure, providing architectural lessons for what International IDEA \citeyearpar{idea2026democracy} calls the ``democracy stack.''

The paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} covers the theoretical background: software architecture quality attributes, software ecosystem research, and democratic theory. Section~\ref{sec:method} describes the method. Section~\ref{sec:analysis} presents the analysis, organized around Decidim (deep case), Consul (comparison case), Polis (contrasting paradigm), and a synthesis of trade-offs. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications for the architecture-as-governance thesis, for SECO research, and for European democratic digital infrastructure. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with limitations and directions for future research.

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Software Architecture for Public Infrastructure}\label{sec:sa}

Software architecture is the set of structures needed to reason about a system --- the elements, the relations between them, and the properties of both \citep{bass2021software}. Architecture decisions shape what a system can and cannot do over its lifetime, mainly through quality attributes: measurable properties like modifiability, interoperability, availability, performance, security, deployability, and testability. In commercial software, these are optimized for business value. When the system is democratic infrastructure, the same reasoning applies but the goal is different: which architecture best supports democratic participation and public legitimacy?

This paper focuses on four quality attributes that are especially important for democratic infrastructure. \emph{Modifiability} is how easily a system can be changed. In democratic infrastructure, it matters because communities with different institutional rules, languages, and participatory traditions need to adapt the platform without hard forking the codebase --- otherwise the architecture limits political pluralism. \emph{Interoperability} is how well a system can exchange data and services with others. It determines whether a platform can work with other democratic tools or stays isolated. \emph{Deployability} is how easily new organizations can adopt and run the platform. A platform that requires a lot of technical expertise to deploy will in practice only be available to well-resourced institutions --- so deployability determines who gets to participate. \emph{Transparency} or auditability does not appear in Bass et al.'s standard taxonomy but is essential here. Citizens need to understand what the system does and how it handles their input. A participatory budgeting platform with opaque vote-counting logic undermines the democratic purpose it is supposed to serve.

These four attributes create trade-offs specific to public infrastructure. Modifiability is in tension with integrity: too-easy modification can fragment the ecosystem. Interoperability is in tension with autonomy: shared standards enable collaboration but can exclude communities whose practices do not fit. Deployability is in tension with customizability: making deployment easy often means reducing options for local adaptation. These tensions are not problems to solve --- they are features of the design space. \citet{heeks2025three} identify three similar structural tensions in public sector digital platforms: openness versus control, stability versus flexibility, and inclusiveness versus efficiency. Their analysis, based on national-scale digital public infrastructure, echoes the trade-offs we describe at the architectural level, suggesting that similar tensions appear as policy dilemmas at the institutional level. The value of an architectural lens is that it makes these tensions concrete: it locates them in specific decisions about module boundaries, API contracts, configuration mechanisms, and deployment pipelines.

\subsection{Software Ecosystems and Democratic Governance}\label{sec:seco}

A software ecosystem (SECO) is ``the interaction of a set of actors on top of a common technological platform, which results in a number of software solutions or services'' \citep{manikas2013systematic}. The concept extends the analysis beyond a single product to include the network of organizations, developers, and users that develop around a shared technical platform. \citet{christensen2014analysis} propose that ecosystems can be analyzed through three structures: the organizational structure (actors and their relationships), the business structure (value flows and governance), and the software structure (the technical platform and its extension mechanisms). In most commercial ecosystems, the business structure dominates --- the platform owner's revenue model shapes both the architecture and the organizational relationships. We hypothesize that in civic tech ecosystems, the software structure has an outsized influence on the other two. Christensen et al.\ do not posit a fixed causal ordering between the structures, and we do not claim a simple reversal. But in the cases we study, decisions about modularity, APIs, and configuration appear to shape which organizations can participate, on what terms, and with how much autonomy. Whether this holds more broadly is an empirical question that our analysis begins to address.

Ecosystem health is a framework for evaluating whether an ecosystem works well. \citet{jansen2014measuring} identifies three dimensions: productivity (how fast the ecosystem generates new functionality), robustness (whether it can survive disruptions, including the loss of key actors), and niche creation (whether it supports diversity by letting new actors find roles). These dimensions connect to democratic values in interesting ways. Niche creation, for example, relates to the concern that diverse communities should have space for meaningful participation. But this connection has not been explored systematically. \citet{iansiti2004strategy} classify ecosystem actors into three roles: keystones (who create value by sharing assets and enabling others), dominators (who capture most of the value), and niche creators (who specialize in narrow segments). These roles map onto civic tech in ways we examine in the analysis: Decidim's technical office operates as a keystone (maintaining shared infrastructure while enabling independent module development), while Consul's Madrid team functioned more as a dominator (controlling the codebase without mechanisms for external contribution).

Despite the clear relevance of ecosystem thinking to civic technology, there is a gap in the literature. Manikas's \citeyearpar{manikas2016revisiting} systematic review covered 231 studies on software ecosystems and found none that looked at commons-based, civic, or democratic platforms. While nearly a decade has passed since that review, we have not found subsequent SECO studies that fill this gap. The ecosystems studied were almost all commercial: app stores, enterprise platforms, proprietary developer ecosystems. \citet{knutas2020local} recognized this gap and proposed applying the OSSECO (open-source software ecosystem) framework to civic tech, but the empirical work was never done. More recently, \citet{noori2025patterns} have studied governance transitions in open-source projects --- the shift from founder-led to community governance --- which is directly relevant to how civic tech platforms mature. Still, the intersection of SECO analysis and democratic digital infrastructure is mostly unexplored. This paper begins to fill that gap.

\subsection{Architecture as Governance: Applying the Thesis to Civic Tech}\label{sec:arch-gov}

As introduced in Section~\ref{sec:sa}, the idea that architecture functions as governance is well established --- from \citet{winner1980artifacts} through \citet{lessig2006code} to \citet{schneider2024governable}. But this literature has focused mostly on online communities (Reddit, Discord, DAOs) and internet infrastructure, not on civic technology platforms used by public institutions.

The work closest to ours comes from within the Decidim project itself. \citet[p.~68]{barandiaran2024decidim} write that ``Decidim must take into account the democratic implications of any technical decision.'' \citet{palacin2024configurations} studied how Decidim's modularity translates into 25 distinct configurations across 31 participatory budgeting cases, asking ``which political values should be enforced by platform design?'' But neither performs formal software architecture analysis. The Plurality framework \citep{weyl2025plurality}, which defines democratic technology as ``technology for collaboration across social difference,'' offers normative vocabulary but no grounding in architectural methods.

Our contribution is to apply the architecture-as-governance insight using software architecture and SECO methods to civic technology --- a domain where this combination has not been tried. The mappings we propose --- modifiability enabling pluralism, transparency enabling legitimacy, interoperability enabling collaboration, deployability enabling access --- are informed by this literature and examined against architectural evidence. They are analytical propositions, not demonstrated causal links. Our evidence shows that the architecture \emph{affords} certain democratic possibilities (e.g., modularity makes it possible for diverse communities to adapt the platform), but we cannot show from architectural analysis alone that these possibilities were actually realized in practice. Establishing that would require deployment studies and stakeholder interviews beyond this paper's scope.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

This study uses a multi-case architectural decision analysis to investigate how software architecture shapes the democratic capacity of civic tech platforms. Instead of treating architecture as a static description, we focus on key design decisions and trace their effects on quality attributes relevant to democratic infrastructure. For each case, the method has four steps: lightweight architectural recovery from primary sources, identification of key architectural decisions, analysis of quality attribute trade-offs, and assessment of ecosystem outcomes.

For each platform, we recover the architecture from primary sources: codebase structure, dependency files, extension mechanisms, API boundaries, and official documentation. This is lightweight recovery, not full formal reconstruction --- it is enough to identify the decisions that shaped each platform without claiming completeness. We then identify key architectural decisions, drawing on the architectural decision record tradition \citep{tyree2005architecture}. We focus on choices between modular and monolithic organization, plugin versus fork extension strategies, integration approaches, and data coupling. For each decision, we trace consequences for quality attributes using the framework of \citet{bass2021software}, mapping decisions to trade-offs in modifiability, interoperability, deployability, and transparency. Finally, we assess ecosystem outcomes using SECO health metrics \citep{manikas2013systematic, jansen2014measuring} together with quantitative GitHub indicators: contributor distribution, fork activity, and module-level commit patterns.

The choice of these four quality attributes --- modifiability, interoperability, deployability, and transparency --- needs justification. \citet{bass2021software} list many more quality attributes, including security, availability, performance, and testability. We selected these four because they are the ones where civic tech platforms most clearly diverge from commercial systems and where the architecture-as-governance literature predicts the strongest political consequences. Security and availability matter for any system. But modifiability determines whether diverse communities can shape the platform \citep{schneider2021modular}, interoperability determines whether platforms can form ecosystems or stay as silos, deployability determines who gets access \citep{heeks2025three}, and transparency determines whether citizens can trust what the system does. Other attributes --- especially security, which protects participants from surveillance --- are important but are not where the architectural divergence between our cases is most visible. This selection is a scoping choice, not a claim that other attributes do not matter. Each mapping from quality attribute to democratic outcome is argued from evidence in the analysis.

The three cases are structured asymmetrically on purpose. Decidim is the deep case and gets full architectural recovery. Its architecture --- 23 Rails engines organized through a manifest and registry pattern within a space-and-component matrix --- is the most architecturally rich civic tech platform available, making it the best case for studying the relationship between architecture and democratic capacity. Consul is the comparison case, with recovery focused on the points where it contrasts with Decidim. The two platforms share an origin in Spain's 15M movement and briefly shared early code, but went in opposite architectural directions: Decidim pursued radical modularity while Consul kept a monolithic structure that depends on forking for customization. This shared origin and divergent evolution provides a comparative case that approximates (but is not) a natural experiment --- the architectural divergence was a deliberate choice shaped by organizational and political factors as well, which we discuss in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}. Polis is a contrasting paradigm rather than a third equal case. Its approach --- algorithmic deliberation through PCA and k-means clustering rather than structured participation processes --- produces a different trade-off profile across the same quality attributes. The Polis analysis is shorter, and it serves to test whether our framework works beyond the participatory platform genre.

Data sources are mainly technical. We use the public GitHub repositories of all three platforms, gemspec and dependency files, architecture documentation, and developer guides. Secondary sources include \citet{barandiaran2024decidim} on Decidim's sociotechnical design, the OSOR case study on Consul's European deployments \citep{linaker2025consul}, the FAccT 2025 paper on Polis, and the Codegram architecture blog on early Decidim design decisions \citep{codegram-decidim}. Ecosystem indicators are derived from GitHub repository metadata.

Several limitations follow. Lightweight recovery does not capture every structural detail and may miss decisions embedded in runtime behavior. GitHub metrics are proxies for ecosystem health, not direct measures of adoption or democratic impact. Three platforms cannot represent all civic tech architectures. Most importantly, our mappings from quality attributes to democratic outcomes are analytical rather than empirically validated through deployment studies. We argue for their plausibility based on architectural evidence and democratic theory, but establishing causal links would require longitudinal field research beyond the scope of this study.

\section{Analysis}\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Decidim: Architecture Recovery}\label{sec:decidim}

Decidim is a modular monolith built with Ruby on Rails. The monorepo at \texttt{github.com/decidim/decidim} contains 23 first-party engines, each packaged as a standalone Ruby gem. This section recovers the architecture from the codebase and documentation, focusing on the decisions that shaped the platform's trajectory.

\paragraph{Engine structure.}
The engines are organized in layers. At the base is \texttt{decidim-core}, which every other engine depends on. Above that are infrastructure engines: \texttt{decidim-admin} (the administration panel), \texttt{decidim-system} (multi-tenant superadmin), \texttt{decidim-api} (GraphQL interface), \texttt{decidim-verifications} (identity verification), \texttt{decidim-forms} (shared form primitives), and \texttt{decidim-comments} (cross-engine commenting). Then come the participatory spaces --- \texttt{decidim-participatory\_processes}, \texttt{decidim-assemblies}, \texttt{decidim-conferences}, \texttt{decidim-initiatives} --- each a separate engine. Finally, the participatory components: \texttt{decidim-proposals}, \texttt{decidim-meetings}, \texttt{decidim-budgets}, \texttt{decidim-surveys}, \texttt{decidim-debates}, \texttt{decidim-blogs}, \texttt{decidim-pages}, \texttt{decidim-accountability}, and \texttt{decidim-sortitions}.

\paragraph{Dependency graph.}
The dependency structure forms a hub-and-spoke pattern. Every engine declares \texttt{add\_dependency "decidim-core"} in its gemspec file. No engine-to-engine dependency bypasses core. \texttt{decidim-admin} and \texttt{decidim-api} serve as secondary hubs. The result is a layered star: core at layer~0, infrastructure engines at layer~1, and space and component engines at layer~2. Figure~\ref{fig:dependency} visualizes this structure.

\paragraph{The space/component matrix.}
This is the key architectural pattern. Spaces and components compose at runtime through a polymorphic association. The \texttt{decidim\_components} table in \texttt{decidim-core} stores a \texttt{participatory\_space\_id} and a \texttt{participatory\_space\_type} (a polymorphic \texttt{belongs\_to}). An administrator can attach any component to any space without writing code --- proposals in a participatory process, budgets in an assembly, debates in a conference. This $N \times M$ composition is Decidim's central architectural innovation. \citet[p.~75]{barandiaran2024decidim} describe it as a ``combinatorial matrix,'' and use the metaphor that ``participatory components are to participatory spaces what furniture is to rooms'' (p.~82). The matrix enables combinatorial participation design without combinatorial code. Figure~\ref{fig:matrix} illustrates this composition.

\paragraph{Four interaction mechanisms.}
The engines interact through four mechanisms that define the internal API boundaries:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \emph{Rails engine mounting.} Each engine uses \texttt{isolate\_namespace} to keep its code separate. Component engines are mounted dynamically under the space's URL scope at runtime.
  \item \emph{Manifest and registry pattern.} \texttt{decidim-core} maintains global registries for components and spaces (\texttt{ComponentManifest}, \texttt{ParticipatorySpaceManifest}). Engines register themselves at boot time by calling \texttt{Decidim.register\_component} or \texttt{Decidim.register\_participatory\_space}. Core reads from these registries to build menus, set up routes, and expose the API.
  \item \emph{Shared database.} All engines write to a single PostgreSQL database. Cross-engine data access happens through ActiveRecord associations, not service calls. This means tight data coupling, but it avoids the overhead of a distributed system.
  \item \emph{Pub/sub events.} \texttt{Decidim::EventsManager.publish} wraps \texttt{ActiveSupport::Notifications}. This lets modules trigger side effects across engines --- for example, notifying followers when a proposal is published --- without creating direct dependencies between the engines.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Third-party extension mechanism.}
Community modules use the same interface as first-party ones. A developer creates a Ruby gem, declares a dependency on \texttt{decidim-core}, and calls \texttt{Decidim.register\_component} in an initializer. The module is installed by adding one line to the deployer's \texttt{Gemfile}. It is auto-discovered through Rails engine loading. A CLI scaffold (\texttt{decidim --component my-component}) generates boilerplate. This design has produced over 80 community-developed modules (listed in the Decidim module directory and published as Ruby gems) that work alongside the core engines.

\paragraph{Quality attribute profile.}
\emph{Modifiability} is high. The engine architecture lets deployers add, remove, or replace components without touching core code. Customization happens through modules, not forks. \emph{Interoperability} is moderate. There is a GraphQL API for data access, and integration choices are deliberately open-source (OpenStreetMap instead of Google Maps, Matomo instead of Google Analytics, Jitsi instead of Zoom). But there are no cross-platform standards for civic tech interoperability. \emph{Deployability} is moderate to low. Setup is complex: a Rails application, module configuration, database, and 23 engines that need to work together. Multi-tenancy helps --- one installation can serve multiple organizations --- but the initial barrier is still high. \emph{Transparency} is high. Proposals are sorted randomly to prevent a ``rich get richer'' dynamic. Administrators cannot delete proposals, which serves as an anti-censorship measure. The project frames its design as ``participant experience (PX)'' rather than ``user experience (UX).'' All code is licensed under AGPL~v3.

\paragraph{Ecosystem indicators.}
As of 2023, Decidim has 135 contributors, 1,265 stars, and 368 forks on GitHub. There have been over 7,600 pull requests and 3,357 feature requests or bug reports. The platform is deployed in over 450 institutions across 30 countries. Barcelona alone has over 120,000 registered participants. However, the technical office consists of only three employees, and the project is critically dependent on Catalan institutional funding \citep{barandiaran2024decidim}. \citet{cobos2025comparison} find that activity ``tends to concentrate on a few contributors,'' raising bus factor concerns. The ecosystem is productive and diverse, but its robustness is fragile.


\subsection{Consul: The Comparison Case}\label{sec:consul}

Consul and Decidim share an origin. Both emerged from Spain's 15M/Indignados movement. Barcelona initially contributed to Consul (which powered decide.madrid.es), but proposed modularizing the codebase. According to the Codegram architecture blog, ``the talks on this document didn't come to a successful end'' \citep{codegram-decidim}. Barcelona then rewrote from scratch, creating Decidim in 2016--2017. The architectural divergence between the two platforms starts here.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
Consul is a single monolithic Rails application. There is no gem separation and no engine isolation. Features --- proposals, debates, budgets, legislation, voting, polls --- all share the same codebase, the same \texttt{ApplicationController}, and the same routes file. The \texttt{config/routes.rb} file defines all feature routes in a single file. Models live in \texttt{app/models/} with functional subdirectories (for example, \texttt{budget/investment.rb}), but there are no module-level namespace boundaries enforced at the code level --- a budget model can freely reference a proposal model without going through any API or registry. All tables sit in one database schema with no migration isolation. There is no \texttt{ARCHITECTURE.md} and no \texttt{Gemfile} separation of features into independent packages. Technical documentation covers installation and customization, but does not describe the architecture formally.

\paragraph{Extension mechanism.}
Consul uses what can be called a ``custom shadow folder'' pattern. The documentation describes a file-override system: to change how a budget investment works, you create \texttt{app/models/custom/budget/investment.rb}, which overrides the original. This is monkey-patching, not module isolation. The practical effect is that customization requires forking the entire repository --- the documentation's ``Getting Started'' step~1 is literally ``Create your fork.'' The deployer then maintains that fork across upgrades manually, resolving conflicts between their custom overrides and upstream changes every release. A \texttt{Gemfile\_custom} convention exists for adding dependencies, but these load into the same process, not as separately versioned components.

\paragraph{The fork problem.}
The OSOR/Interoperable Europe case study found ``fragmented development, with multiple forks adapted to different local needs\ldots\ difficult to synchronise updates and maintain security across installations'' \citep{linaker2025consul}. It also reports that ``some expressed the belief that Consul felt like it was being forked by each local government\ldots\ with little getting contributed back upstream.'' According to the NTARI forks report \citep{ntari2025consul}, there are roughly 250 global implementations, most maintaining separate repositories with local modifications that rarely flow back.

\paragraph{Democratic consequence.}
Consul's low modifiability forced a binary choice: accept the platform as-is, or fork it entirely. Barcelona chose to fork and then rewrite. The 250 other deployers mostly forked with local modifications that never came back upstream. In SECO terms, this means loss of collective learning, duplicated effort, and reduced robustness. The architecture did not just constrain the code --- it constrained who could participate in shaping the platform.


\subsection{Polis: The Contrasting Paradigm}\label{sec:polis}

Polis takes a fundamentally different approach. It does not solve the same problem as Decidim and Consul (structured participation processes). Instead, it synthesizes opinions through algorithmic deliberation. It is included not as a third case in the same comparison, but as evidence of a different trade-off profile.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
Polis has a Clojure backend and a JavaScript frontend. It is monolithic but algorithmically specialized. The core innovation is a pipeline: participants vote agree, disagree, or pass on statements, producing a vote matrix. Principal component analysis (PCA) reduces this matrix to two dimensions, creating an ``opinion landscape.'' K-means clustering (with $k$ selected by silhouette coefficient) identifies groups of like-minded voters. The system then finds ``bridging statements'' that resonate across divided groups and ``consensus statements'' agreed on by all clusters. This algorithm is the product --- everything else is infrastructure around it.

\paragraph{The different trade-off.}
Polis trades ecosystem breadth for algorithmic depth. There are few third-party modules and a thin contributor ecosystem (around 50 contributors, roughly 130 forks on GitHub, about 517 stars). But the tightly coupled architecture ensures the deliberation algorithm works as designed. Docker-based deployment makes it relatively easy to run. However, deployers can run it without understanding --- or being able to audit --- the PCA algorithm that determines what counts as consensus.

\paragraph{Quality attribute profile.}
\emph{Modifiability} is low: tight coupling around the core algorithm and no plugin system. \emph{Deployability} is high: Docker containers make spinning up an instance straightforward. \emph{Transparency} is low to moderate: the code is open source, but the algorithmic core --- dimensionality reduction and clustering --- is opaque to most users and deployers. The opinion landscape visualization is readable, but the mechanism producing it is not.

\paragraph{Democratic implication.}
Polis shows that architectural decisions about algorithms are also governance decisions. The PCA + k-means approach embeds an epistemological assumption: that consensus is findable through dimensionality reduction of opinion space. This is not neutral. It determines what kinds of agreement become visible and what kinds remain hidden. \citet{bono2025artificial} identify algorithmic transparency as a key design principle for AI in participation platforms --- a concern that Polis predates but does not fully address.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Architecture as Governance in Practice}\label{sec:disc-gov}

The architecture-as-governance thesis is well established --- from \citet{winner1980artifacts} through \citet{lessig2006code} to \citet{schneider2024governable}. Our analysis shows how it plays out in practice in civic technology.

Decidim's modular architecture did not just make the code flexible --- it made it possible for diverse communities to shape the platform without needing permission from the core team. Any organization can build a module, publish it as a gem, and have it work alongside the official engines. This resembles what \citet{schneider2021modular} call ``modular politics'' --- composable governance primitives implemented as software. Schneider et al.\ envision governance rules that can be mixed and matched; Decidim achieves something analogous at the participation-design level, where spaces and components can be combined freely. The parallel is architectural, not exact --- Decidim's modules are participation features, not governance rules per se. Consul's monolith, by contrast, concentrated decision-making. If you wanted to change how the platform works, you either convinced the Madrid team or you forked. In practice, most deployers forked, and the changes stayed local. \citet{schneider2024governable} argues that platform defaults encode governance --- Consul's defaults encoded centralization, even though nobody intended it.

Polis shows a different version of the same pattern. The PCA + k-means algorithm is a decision about what counts as consensus. It is not technically wrong --- it is a reasonable approach to finding common ground in large groups. But it is also not neutral. It makes certain kinds of agreement visible (clusters that converge on bridging statements) and other kinds invisible (minority positions that do not form clean clusters). As \citet{bowker1999sorting} argue, classification systems are political because every category privileges one view and silences another. The same is true for dimensionality reduction on opinion space.

This extends the SECO literature into new territory. \citet{christensen2014analysis} describe how software structure shapes organizational and business structures. Our cases show that this relationship is especially strong in civic tech, where the political consequences of architectural choices are explicit. The Decidim/Consul divergence is the clearest example: the same starting point, the same political movement, but opposite architectural decisions produced radically different ecosystems. This parallels what \citet{noori2025patterns} find in open-source projects more broadly --- that governance transitions are constrained by early architectural choices. Civic tech is no different, except that the stakes are democratic rather than commercial.

\subsection{What SECO Research Gains from Democratic Analysis}\label{sec:disc-seco}

The SECO health dimensions from \citet{jansen2014measuring} --- productivity, robustness, niche creation --- offer useful organizing concepts for civic tech, though we apply them as qualitative lenses rather than with their full operationalized measurement frameworks (which would require longitudinal data on application survival rates, revenue flows, and developer retention that we do not have). At this coarser level: Decidim's 80+ community modules suggest a productive ecosystem; Consul's fragmentation into diverging forks suggests low robustness (the ecosystem cannot easily absorb disruptions or share improvements); Polis's thin contributor base suggests limited niche creation. But even a fully operationalized health assessment would miss something important: it would not tell us whether the architecture actually enables the democratic participation it claims to support.

A civic tech ecosystem can look ``healthy'' by standard metrics while being architecturally hostile to democratic participation. Consul had a growing contributor base and many deployments, but the extension mechanism forced adopters to maintain separate forks. By productivity measures, it looked fine. By democratic measures --- whether diverse communities can meaningfully shape the platform --- it failed.

The architecture-as-governance lens adds an evaluative layer: not just ``is the ecosystem growing?'' but ``does the architecture enable the participation it claims to support?'' --- what \citet{palacin2024configurations} frame as ``which political values should be enforced by platform design.'' This goes beyond civic tech. Any mission-driven ecosystem --- health technology, education, environmental monitoring --- faces similar tensions. \citet{koskinen2020understanding} show this in DHIS2, the health information platform used in 80+ countries, where data model decisions determine what health authorities can count and report.

\subsection{Implications for the Democracy Stack}\label{sec:disc-stack}

International IDEA \citeyearpar{idea2026democracy} proposes building a ``democracy stack'' --- digital public infrastructure designed to support democratic rights and participation. \citet{ceps2025building} advocates for interoperable European digital public infrastructure. But neither specifies what good architecture looks like in this context.

Our findings suggest several concrete principles. First, democratic DPI needs modular architecture, not monolithic. The Decidim/Consul comparison shows why: modularity enables diverse communities to adapt the platform, while monoliths force forking and fragmentation. Second, interoperability standards should be open, but the interoperability/autonomy trade-off needs careful attention. Standards that do not account for local variation can exclude the communities they are meant to serve. Third, deployability matters for access, but should not come at the cost of transparency. Polis is easy to deploy but hard to audit. Fourth, the governance structure should match the architectural distribution of power. Decidim's community governance (the Decidim Association, Metadecidim) reflects the distributed nature of its technical architecture.

\subsection{Evaluating the Approach}\label{sec:evaluation}

Applying software architecture and SECO methods to civic tech revealed both strengths and limitations of the approach, which are worth reflecting on.

The framework worked best where architectural decisions had clearly documented consequences. The Decidim/Consul divergence is the strongest case precisely because the causal chain is traceable: a proposed modularization was rejected, a fork happened, different architectures were built, and different ecosystem outcomes followed. This is the kind of evidence that architectural analysis is designed to handle. But the causal claim should be qualified. The divergence between Decidim and Consul was not only architectural --- it was also organizational and political. Barcelona and Madrid had different governance cultures, different levels of institutional support, and different political trajectories (Madrid's Ahora Madrid government lost power in 2019, which weakened Consul's institutional backing). The architecture mattered, but it was not the only factor. Disentangling architectural from organizational causes would require interview-based research that was beyond this study's scope.

The four quality attributes captured the most visible trade-offs, but the framework may have missed important tensions that sit at a different level. Security, for example, did not appear as a major axis of variation across our three cases, but it is critical for platforms that handle verified identities and votes --- Decidim's ElectionGuard integration for end-to-end verifiable elections suggests it matters more than our framework highlights. Similarly, the framework focused on the platform level and said little about the deployment and operations level, where questions of hosting, data sovereignty, and vendor dependence may be equally consequential for democratic outcomes.

Lightweight architectural recovery was sufficient for this study --- it identified the key decisions and their trade-offs --- but it has limits. The Decidim analysis drew on gemspec files, code structure, and detailed documentation, which gave confidence in the findings. The Consul analysis relied more heavily on the OSOR case study and documentation, since the monolithic structure offers fewer formal architectural boundaries to recover. The Polis analysis was the thinnest, relying mostly on published descriptions of the algorithm rather than deep code-level recovery. A study with more time could do formal dependency analysis and commit-pattern mining to strengthen the Consul and Polis cases.

Finally, the GitHub ecosystem metrics used in this paper --- contributor counts, fork numbers, stars --- are proxies, not direct measures of ecosystem health. Fork counts in particular are ambiguous: for Consul, a fork may represent a genuine local deployment, an abandoned experiment, or a one-time contribution attempt. The high fork count does not automatically mean 250 active diverging implementations. A more rigorous ecosystem analysis would distinguish active from inactive forks and track upstream contribution rates over time.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper asked what software architecture trade-offs builders of democratic digital infrastructure must navigate. Through a multi-case architectural decision analysis of Decidim, Consul, and Polis, we identified four trade-offs that are both technical and political: modifiability versus sustainability (Decidim's modules enable pluralism but cost maintainability; Consul's monolith was simpler but forced fragmentation through forking), interoperability versus autonomy (open integration choices help but no shared civic tech standards exist), deployability versus transparency (Polis is easy to deploy but its algorithm is hard to audit), and algorithmic sophistication versus ecosystem breadth (Polis's tight coupling produces powerful output but a thin ecosystem).

These are not abstract observations. If Copenhagen, or any European city, wanted to build democratic participation infrastructure tomorrow, these are the trade-offs they would face. A decade of civic tech experience shows what happens when you navigate them in one direction versus another.

The architecture-as-governance thesis is well established in STS, law, and platform studies \citep{winner1980artifacts, lessig2006code, schneider2024governable}. This paper shows what it looks like in practice when applied to civic technology through software architecture and SECO methods. The Decidim/Consul divergence --- same origin, opposite architecture, radically different ecosystem outcomes --- is the clearest demonstration. This finding connects software architecture research to the emerging European conversation about democratic digital public infrastructure. It also enriches SECO research with a normative dimension: the health of a civic tech ecosystem should be evaluated not just by productivity and robustness, but by whether the architecture actually supports the democratic values it claims to serve.

\paragraph{Returning to Denmark.}
Borgerforslag.dk made every trade-off in the direction of simplicity: closed-source, single-purpose, no ecosystem. That works for a petition tool. But if Denmark wants participation infrastructure that lets diverse communities deliberate, adapt, and govern, the architectural lessons from a decade of civic tech suggest very different choices would be needed. The architecture is not a technical detail to be decided in procurement. It is the democratic decision.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
This study relies on secondary sources and lightweight architectural recovery rather than formal reconstruction. GitHub metrics are proxies, not direct measures of democratic impact. Three platforms, however carefully selected, cannot represent all civic tech architectures --- the field includes at least 25 platforms with genuine architectural variety (from Django-based Adhocracy to LiquidFeedback's database-as-application approach). The mappings from quality attributes to democratic outcomes are analytical claims, not empirically validated through deployment studies.

\paragraph{Future research.}
Two directions are promising, and both could serve as the basis for a master's thesis building on this project. First, an empirical study of how specific architectural decisions changed governance dynamics over time --- combining repository mining, architectural analysis, and stakeholder interviews with Decidim deployers and contributors. This would address the causal limitations discussed in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation} by grounding the architecture-governance connection in direct evidence from practitioners. Second, a design-oriented approach: what would a democratic participation architecture for a Nordic municipality actually look like, given these trade-offs? A design science approach could test the principles identified here against real institutional requirements.
