% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Europe is investing in democratic digital infrastructure: the Digital Services Act, the European Digital Identity framework, IDEA's ``democracy stack'' framing, and CEPS's interoperability advocacy. Policy names values, not architecture. Civic technology platforms have been building this kind of infrastructure for over a decade, yet they have rarely been analysed through software architecture and software ecosystem (SECO) methods. This paper proposes a small analytical framework for reasoning about software architecture decisions in democratic digital infrastructure. The framework has three parts. First, four quality attributes that connect architecture to democratic capacities: modifiability, interoperability, configurability, and transparency and auditability. Second, the three-structures lens of Christensen et al.~2014, where ecosystem health depends on the fit between organisational, business, and software structures. Third, a set of architectural trade-offs derived from the four attributes.

The framework is demonstrated through a comparison of Decidim and Consul, two civic tech platforms with shared origins in Spain's 15M movement and opposite architectural paths. The application uses architectural recovery, evolutionary analysis, and a fork-divergence study across 1{,}030 GitHub forks. Decidim's modular software fits its coordinating Association and consultancy-anchored funding base. Consul's monolithic software requires a coordinating capacity that its small Foundation and shoestring funding cannot provide, and this misalignment shows up as fork fragmentation. The paper proposes \emph{fork-divergence shape} --- the pattern of forks across the ahead-of-upstream and behind-upstream quadrants --- as an initial diagnostic for whether a civic-tech ecosystem extends or fragments. The two-case scope means that quality attributes such as security, accessibility, and sovereignty, which Decidim and Consul do not strongly differentiate, are left for future work.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Europe is investing in democratic digital infrastructure. The Digital Services Act regulates online platforms. The European Digital Identity framework sets up shared digital identity. International IDEA argues for a ``democracy stack'' of software that supports participation and deliberation across Europe \citep{idea2026democracy}. CEPS argues for European Digital Public Infrastructure focused on interoperability and sovereignty \citep{ceps2025building}.

These policy proposals name what they want. They do not say what the software architecture should look like.

Civic technology platforms have been building this kind of software for over a decade. Decidim is used by over 400 institutions \citep{barandiaran2024decidim}. Consul is used by over 100 municipalities \citep{linaker2025consul}. The platforms exist and the data is there. What remains under-analysed is how they were built, how their architectures shaped their ecosystems, and why two platforms with similar origins ended up so different.

This paper asks: \emph{what software architecture decisions matter for democratic digital infrastructure, and how can they be analysed through software architecture and software ecosystem methods?}

The methods needed to approach this question are available. Software architecture research studies quality attributes and trade-offs \citep{bass2021software}. Software ecosystem (SECO) research studies ecosystem health and governance \citep{manikas2016revisiting, christensen2014analysis, jansen2014measuring, iansiti2004strategy}. Work in STS and platform studies shows that architectural decisions participate in governance by making some forms of action, coordination, and control easier than others \citep{winner1980artifacts, lessig2006code, denardis2014global, schneider2024governable}. These perspectives have rarely been combined to analyse civic technology platforms as democratic digital infrastructure. \citet{manikas2016revisiting}'s 2016 review of 231 SECO studies found no work on civic or commons-based platforms. \citet{knutas2020local} proposed applying open-source ecosystem analysis to civic tech but did not carry out the empirical analysis. \citet{palacin2024configurations} come closest: they study how Decidim's modular design produces 25 different platform configurations, but they do not analyse the architecture itself as part of a software ecosystem.

This paper proposes a small analytical framework for thinking about software architecture decisions in democratic digital infrastructure. The framework brings together four quality attributes that connect architecture choices to democratic capacities (modifiability, interoperability, configurability, and transparency and auditability), \citet{christensen2014analysis}'s three-structures lens (organisational, business, and software structures, where ecosystem health depends on the three fitting together), and a set of trade-offs derived from the four attributes.

We apply the framework to two civic technology platforms: Decidim and Consul. Both started around Spain's 15M movement in 2015--2016. They took opposite architectural paths. Decidim went modular, with 27 Ruby engines and a coordinating Association. Consul stayed monolithic, with deployers adapting the platform through forks and a small Foundation coordinating from the outside. We use architectural recovery from gemspec files and source code, commit history analysis, and a study of 1{,}030 Consul forks. We propose \emph{fork-divergence shape} as an initial diagnostic for distinguishing ecosystem extension from fork fragmentation in civic tech ecosystems.

We do not claim to invent the components. The quality attributes come from standard software architecture literature; the three-structures lens is Christensen et al.'s; the architecture-as-governance thesis is well established. The contribution of this paper is to bring these components together for civic tech, demonstrate the framework on two worked cases, and surface fork-divergence shape as a diagnostic that emerged from the application. Decidim and Consul are the first worked cases in this paper, not a full validation of the framework across the field. A wider programme would need to look at quality attributes our two cases do not strongly differentiate, such as security, accessibility, and sovereignty, and to evaluate the approach against more cases.

Section~\ref{sec:background} covers the literature on software architecture, software ecosystems, and architecture-as-governance. Section~\ref{sec:method} describes our method. Section~\ref{sec:framework} presents the framework. Section~\ref{sec:application} applies it to Decidim and Consul. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses what the framework shows and what it does not. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

This paper draws on three literatures. Software architecture research gives us quality attributes and trade-offs as a way of reasoning about design decisions. Software ecosystem research gives us a way of analysing the organisational, business, and technical structures around a platform. Architecture-as-governance literature shows that software architectures participate in governance by making some forms of action, coordination, and control easier than others. We need all three to reason about democratic digital infrastructure.

\subsection{Software Architecture Quality Attributes}\label{sec:sa}

We start with the standard definition. \citet[p.~2]{bass2021software} call software architecture ``the set of structures needed to reason about a system.'' Architecture decisions affect a system's properties over its lifetime through quality attributes: measurable properties like modifiability, performance, security, and so on. Quality attributes are useful here because they let us evaluate architecture without reducing it to implementation details. They allow us to ask not only what a platform contains, but what kinds of change, inspection, integration, and adaptation its structure makes easier or harder.

In commercial software, quality attributes are usually optimised for business value. For democratic digital infrastructure the goal is different: which architectural properties support democratic participation, public legitimacy, and institutional adaptability?

We focus on four quality attributes. \emph{Modifiability} is how easily the system can be changed. Civic tech platforms have to adapt to communities with different rules, languages, and participatory traditions; modifiable platforms can do this without forcing each deployer to maintain a separate fork. \emph{Interoperability} is how well the system exchanges data and services with others; it matters because no single platform will serve all democratic functions, so platforms need to compose. \emph{Configurability} is the platform's support for configuring varied participation processes (proposals, voting, deliberation, accountability) without changing the source code. PolicyKit \citep{zhang2020policykit} formalised this property for online community governance. \emph{Transparency and auditability} is how much of the platform's operation can be inspected, logged, and verified by relevant observers. Democratic legitimacy depends partly on the ability to audit and contest outcomes, although technical auditability does not by itself guarantee that citizens can meaningfully use these mechanisms in practice.

Other quality attributes (security, accessibility, sovereignty, performance) matter for democratic infrastructure too. We do not treat them as primary axes here and return to this scoping in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Software Ecosystems}\label{sec:seco}

SECO research broadens the unit of analysis from the software to the wider system around it. \citet{manikas2013systematic} define a software ecosystem (SECO) as ``the interaction of a set of actors on top of a common technological platform, which results in a number of software solutions or services.'' The concept extends beyond a single product to the organisations, developers, and users that develop around a shared platform.

\citet{christensen2014analysis} propose that ecosystems can be analysed through three interacting structures: an \emph{organisational} structure (who participates, in what roles, how they coordinate), a \emph{business} structure (who pays, who captures value, what incentives flow), and a \emph{software} structure (the platform architecture, extension mechanisms, APIs). Their argument is that ecosystems thrive when these three structures fit together and struggle when they do not. We use this lens in Section~\ref{sec:application} to compare Decidim and Consul.

Other SECO tools we use are \citet{iansiti2004strategy}'s ecosystem actor classification (keystones, dominators, niche creators) and \citet{jansen2014measuring}'s ecosystem health dimensions (productivity, robustness, niche creation). In this paper, these concepts are treated as analytical lenses rather than direct measurements of democratic value.

The gap in SECO research for civic tech is visible in the existing literature. \citet{manikas2016revisiting}'s systematic review of 231 SECO studies found none on commons-based, civic, or democratic platforms. \citet{knutas2020local} proposed applying OSSECO methods to civic tech but did not carry out the empirical work. \citet{palacin2024configurations} study Decidim's modularity from a human-computer interaction perspective, but they do not analyse the architecture itself as part of a software ecosystem.

\subsection{Architecture as Governance}\label{sec:archgov}

Software architecture as a form of governance is a well-established position in science and technology studies (STS) and platform studies. \citet{winner1980artifacts} argued that artifacts can embody political relations. \citet{lessig2006code} extended this to digital systems: software architecture is one of four modalities of regulation, alongside law, norms, and markets. \citet{denardis2014global} argues that arrangements of technical architecture are arrangements of power. \citet{schneider2024governable} traces ``implicit feudalism'' through online platforms: many platforms make admins into benevolent dictators by design.

This thesis is analytical rather than deterministic. It does not mean that architecture alone causes political outcomes. It means that architecture distributes possibilities: it makes some forms of participation, oversight, adaptation, and control easier, and others harder. Much of the existing work reads systems backward to expose their politics. Design-oriented extensions are thinner. The clearest is \citet{schneider2021modular}'s Modular Politics, which proposes four design principles (modularity, expressiveness, portability, interoperability) for a governance layer in online communities. These ideas are directly relevant to civic technology platforms, but they have rarely been combined with software architecture and SECO methods.

This paper sits at the intersection of three literatures that have each established something important but have not been systematically brought together for civic tech. Section~\ref{sec:method} describes how we do that.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

This study uses Design Science Research \citep{hevner2004design, peffers2007design} as its methodological frame. In DSR terms, the problem relevance is the lack of architecture-level reasoning in debates on democratic digital infrastructure. The artefact is the analytical framework presented in Section~\ref{sec:framework}. The demonstration is the Decidim/Consul comparison in Section~\ref{sec:application}. The evaluation remains limited: the paper examines whether the framework can produce a coherent and empirically grounded reading of two cases, but does not validate it across the civic-tech field. We therefore treat the artefact as a Type-1 contribution at \emph{demonstration} maturity: we present the framework and apply it to one comparison, leaving full evaluation across more cases as future work.

\paragraph{Case selection.}
We selected Decidim and Consul as the worked application for two reasons. First, both have publicly developed codebases, active deployments, and enough documentation for architectural recovery. Second, the two platforms share an origin in Spain's 15M movement in 2015--2016 but took opposite architectural paths: Decidim went modular (27 Rails engines, gem-based extension); Consul stayed monolithic (single Rails app, fork-based extension). The shared origin reduces some of the variance that usually confounds case comparisons in software studies, while the architectural divergence provides the contrast we want to study. Other civic tech platforms (Polis, Loomio, CitizenLab, vTaiwan, Adhocracy) are not analysed; a single two-platform comparison is enough to demonstrate the framework at this stage, but not enough to generalise across civic tech as a whole.

\paragraph{Architectural recovery.}
For each platform we recover the software structure from primary sources: codebase organisation, gemspec files, extension mechanisms, and documentation. For Decidim, a script parses all 27 first-party gemspecs at a pinned snapshot, extracts every \texttt{add\_dependency} call, classifies each as internal or external, and identifies \texttt{Decidim.register\_component} and \texttt{Decidim.register\_participatory\_space} calls. The engine dependency graph in Section~\ref{sec:application} is recovered from this data, not asserted from documentation. Consul's recovery is structural: a Rails monolith with a file-override extension mechanism. The recovery captures dependency structure and extension mechanisms, not runtime behaviour or deployment-specific customisations.

\paragraph{Evolutionary analysis.}
For both platforms we extract commit metadata with \texttt{git log --no-merges} on partial clones. Bot authors (Dependabot, Crowdin, Weblate, GitHub Actions, named release bots) are filtered by email and name patterns. We compute total human commits, unique authors, top-three and top-ten contributor share, Gini coefficient, and the per-year commit timeline; for Decidim we also extract per-engine commit counts. We use these as indicators of ecosystem activity and concentration \citep{manikas2013systematic, jansen2014measuring}. They are not treated as direct measures of ecosystem health or democratic value: commit counts show activity, not necessarily meaningful maintenance; author counts show participation, not necessarily influence; and concentration metrics show distribution, not governance quality.

\paragraph{Logical coupling.}
Following \citet{gall1998detection}, we measure which Decidim engines change together. For each non-merge non-bot commit we identify the engines touched and compute Jaccard similarity (commits touching both / commits touching either) for each engine pair. Mega-commits touching more than eight engines are excluded as refactor or release commits that would otherwise dominate the signal. This tests whether the manifest pattern is reflected in change isolation at the repository level, not only in the gemspec dependency structure.

\paragraph{Fork divergence.}
For Consul we use the GitHub REST API to enumerate all forks of \texttt{consuldemocracy/consuldemocracy} (formerly \texttt{consul/consul}) and call the compare endpoint for each, returning commits ahead and behind upstream master. Each fork is classified as \emph{modified} or \emph{unmodified} (commits ahead) and \emph{active} or \emph{dormant} (pushed in the last three years). This analysis covers public forks only and may miss private production forks. Decidim is not subject to this analysis because its documented extension model is based on gem plugins rather than deployment forks.

\paragraph{Three-structures application.}
For each platform we describe the three structures from \citet{christensen2014analysis} --- organisational, business, software --- using primary documentation (Decidim Association governance documents, MetaDecidim assembly records, Consul Democracy Foundation website, OSOR case studies) and the empirical material from architectural recovery and evolutionary analysis. We then assess alignment or misalignment between the three structures. This assessment is interpretive: it asks whether the software structure appears to afford the organisational and business reality around it, and whether the organisational and business structures appear able to sustain the architectural costs. It does not establish causal proof.

\paragraph{Quality-attribute assessment.}
The four quality attributes are assessed qualitatively as low, moderate, or high. The ratings are based on observable architectural mechanisms and supporting evidence from the repository and documentation. For example, modifiability is rated higher when extension is possible without changing core code or maintaining a deployment fork; configurability is rated higher when administrators can compose participation processes through settings rather than code changes; interoperability is rated higher when the platform exposes stable APIs or shared standards; and transparency and auditability are rated higher when logging, versioning, permissions, and public inspection mechanisms are implemented as first-class architectural concerns. The ratings are therefore structured judgements, not measurements.

\paragraph{Data sources.}
Primary sources are the public GitHub repositories at pinned snapshots: \texttt{decidim/decidim} at \texttt{be39c244} (2026-05-12) and \texttt{consuldemocracy/consuldemocracy} at \texttt{3c3b5c4a} (2026-05-11). Secondary sources include \citet{barandiaran2024decidim} on Decidim's sociotechnical design, the OSOR case study on Consul's European deployments \citep{linaker2025consul}, the NTARI fork report \citep{ntari2025consul}, and the Codegram architecture blog on early Decidim design \citep{codegram-decidim}. Reproducibility scripts and findings logs are kept alongside the paper source.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The main limitation, expanded in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}, is that our QA-to-democracy mappings are analytical, not empirically validated through user studies or deployment outcomes. Architectural recovery captures dependency structure and extension mechanisms, not runtime behaviour. Evolutionary metrics use email-based author identity, which conflates authors with multiple emails. Fork analysis covers public forks only. The two-case design demonstrates the framework but does not validate it across the wider civic-tech field.

\section{Framework}\label{sec:framework}

This section presents the framework. It has three components: a set of quality attributes mapped to democratic capacities (Section~\ref{sec:framework-qas}), the three-structures lens for analysing software ecosystems (Section~\ref{sec:framework-ts}), and a derived set of architectural trade-offs (Section~\ref{sec:framework-tradeoffs}). A separate methodological contribution, fork-divergence shape as a niche-creation diagnostic, is described in Section~\ref{sec:framework-fd}. Section~\ref{sec:framework-using} states the procedure for applying the framework to a new platform.

\subsection{Quality Attributes Mapped to Democratic Capacities}\label{sec:framework-qas}

Each of the four quality attributes introduced in Section~\ref{sec:sa} connects an architecture choice to a democratic capacity the platform can support. Each one also pairs with an architectural cost the platform has to pay if it chooses that direction. Table~\ref{tab:qa-mapping} collects the mappings.

\begin{table}[!htbp]
\centering
\caption{Quality attributes mapped to democratic capacities and the architectural costs they impose.}
\label{tab:qa-mapping}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{lXX}
\toprule
\textbf{Quality attribute} & \textbf{Architectural cost} & \textbf{Democratic capacity} \\
\midrule
Modifiability & Sustainability burden of $N$ composable parts & Pluralism: diverse communities can adapt the platform \\
\midrule
Interoperability & Reduced local autonomy under shared standards & Composability: platforms can join wider stacks \\
\midrule
Configurability & Configuration surface complexity; maintenance overhead & Procedural pluralism: participation can be organised in different ways \\
\midrule
Transparency and auditability & Audit infrastructure across all models: immutable logs, change versioning, permission and visibility gating, API discipline & Legitimacy, accountability, and contestability can be supported through inspection and verification \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

The mappings are analytical, not causal proofs. They should not be read as claiming that these quality attributes automatically produce democratic outcomes. Rather, they identify the democratic capacities that each attribute can support when matched by appropriate organisational and institutional conditions. A system whose architecture supports modifiability, for example, creates the conditions for institutional pluralism, but does not guarantee that pluralism will emerge in practice. We therefore treat the mappings as normative design hypotheses that the application in Section~\ref{sec:application} demonstrates against two specific platforms.

Other mappings are possible. Transparency can be read as a precondition for surveillance as well as for accountability; configurability can be read as administrator capture as well as procedural pluralism; interoperability under shared standards can entrench dominant platforms rather than enabling composition. The mappings we use are not the only defensible reading. They are the readings that match our two cases and the democratic capacities the platforms themselves frame their work against. A different corpus or a different normative starting point would land on different mappings, which is part of why we treat this as a small analytical framework rather than a fixed taxonomy.

Configurability and modifiability sit next to each other in the table but address different audiences. Modifiability is about whether the platform can be changed at the code level (a developer's question); configurability is about whether an administrator can compose different participatory processes from the existing components without writing code (an operator's question). Both matter for democratic infrastructure, but they reach different actors and support different democratic capacities.

\subsection{The Three-Structures Lens}\label{sec:framework-ts}

The QAs above describe properties of the software structure alone. \citet{christensen2014analysis}'s three-structures framework gives us a way to think about how the software structure relates to the broader ecosystem.

A software ecosystem has three structures. The \emph{organisational} structure is the people and institutions that participate, their roles, and how they coordinate. The \emph{business} structure is who pays, who captures value, and what incentives flow. The \emph{software} structure is the platform architecture, extension mechanisms, and APIs. Christensen et al. argue that ecosystems thrive when these three structures fit each other and struggle when they do not.

This lens does two things for us. First, it locates the QAs: the four attributes above are properties of the software structure, but their consequences play out in the organisational and business structures. Modifiability matters for pluralism only if the organisational structure includes contributors who are able to use the extension mechanisms. Transparency matters for legitimacy only if there are actors (communities, regulators, deployers, researchers) able to inspect and act on what they see. Second, it identifies a failure mode beyond bad architecture: \emph{misalignment} between architecture and the organisational and business reality around it. A modular architecture without a coordinating body to review, integrate, and maintain contributions may produce fragmentation rather than pluralism. A modifiable platform without sustained funding for maintenance may produce decay rather than adaptation.

\subsection{Architectural Trade-offs}\label{sec:framework-tradeoffs}

The four QAs cannot all be optimised without cost. Each one pairs with an architectural burden (Table~\ref{tab:qa-mapping}), and choosing one direction means accepting that burden elsewhere in the ecosystem.

Three pairings are particularly visible in civic tech and form the framework's architectural trade-offs:

\begin{description}
  \item[Modifiability against sustainability burden.] Modular architectures let many communities adapt the platform; the cost is maintaining many parts. Monolithic architectures can be easier to maintain centrally; the cost is that adaptation often requires forking or direct source modification unless other extension mechanisms are provided.
  \item[Interoperability against reduced autonomy.] Interoperable platforms compose into wider stacks; the cost is constraints on local choice under shared standards. Stand-alone platforms preserve local autonomy; the cost is isolation.
  \item[Configurability against maintenance overhead.] Highly configurable platforms support many different participation processes without code changes; the cost is configuration surface complexity and the maintenance of options across versions. Less configurable platforms are simpler; the cost is that adapting to a new process may require code changes.
\end{description}

Each trade-off is not only an engineering choice but also a governance choice. The architecture makes some forms of adaptation, coordination, and oversight easier than others, whether or not the builders frame the decision politically. The framework makes these choices explicit.

Transparency and auditability is treated separately. Unlike the three above, the cost of transparency (audit infrastructure) does not pull against another democratic capacity directly; it is a cost the platform pays for legitimacy, accountability, and contestability all at once. We discuss it as a primary axis in Section~\ref{sec:application} but do not pair it with another QA as a trade-off.

\subsection{Fork-Divergence Shape: A Niche-Creation Diagnostic}\label{sec:framework-fd}

Standard SECO niche-creation metrics count contributions. They do not necessarily distinguish healthy ecosystem extension from silo formation. We propose \emph{fork-divergence shape} --- the distribution of public forks across ahead-of-upstream and behind-upstream states --- as a niche-creation diagnostic for civic tech platforms that rely on forks as an extension mechanism.

Operationally, each fork can be located in one of four states. \emph{In-sync forks} are neither ahead of nor behind upstream. \emph{Behind-only forks} have no local commits but have fallen behind upstream; these usually represent passive copies or abandoned deployment bases. \emph{Ahead-only forks} have local commits while remaining in sync with upstream; these are candidates for healthy ecosystem extension because local work has not drifted away from the shared codebase. \emph{Ahead-and-behind forks} have both local commits and missing upstream commits; these are candidates for diverged silos because local development and upstream development have separated.

The shape of the fork population, not the total count, is the diagnostic. Healthy ecosystem extension should populate the ahead-only region: deployers keep up with upstream and add their own work, producing changes that upstream can in principle absorb. Silo formation should populate the behind-only and ahead-and-behind regions: either dormant copies fall behind, or active deployments accumulate local changes while also missing upstream changes. We apply this to Consul in Section~\ref{sec:application-consul} and find an empty ahead-only region, which we interpret as a fragmentation signal.

\subsection{Using the Framework}\label{sec:framework-using}

The framework is applied to a civic tech platform in five steps. First, recover the software structure from primary sources: codebase organisation, gemspec or equivalent manifests, extension mechanisms, APIs, and documentation. Second, identify the organisational structure (who participates and how they coordinate) and the business structure (who pays, who captures value, what incentives flow) from governance documents, funding records, and secondary case studies. Third, profile the four quality attributes against this evidence, rating each as low, moderate, or high with the specific architectural choices or data that justify the rating. Fourth, assess whether the three structures fit each other: does the software architecture afford the organisational reality, and does the business structure sustain the architectural costs? Fifth, if the platform's deployers use forks rather than plugins or modules, plot the fork population across the ahead-of-upstream and behind-upstream states to read niche-creation health.

The low/moderate/high ratings are structured judgements rather than measurements. \emph{Modifiability} is rated higher when deployers can extend the platform without modifying core code or maintaining long-lived forks. \emph{Interoperability} is rated higher when the platform exposes stable APIs, shared data formats, or standards that allow composition with other systems. \emph{Configurability} is rated higher when administrators can compose varied participation processes through settings rather than code changes. \emph{Transparency and auditability} is rated higher when logging, versioning, permissioning, and public inspection mechanisms are implemented as first-class architectural concerns rather than narrow administrative features.

The output is a structured comparison the framework's tables can hold (Table~\ref{tab:qa-mapping} for the QA profile, Table~\ref{tab:threestructure} for the three structures). The next section walks through this procedure for Decidim and Consul.

\section{Application: Decidim and Consul}\label{sec:application}

This section applies the framework to Decidim and Consul. For each platform we describe the three structures (organisational, business, software), profile the four quality attributes, and connect the observations to the trade-offs and the architecture-as-governance thesis. The analysis treats repository data as indicators of activity, concentration, and divergence, not as direct measures of democratic value. A cross-case synthesis follows in Section~\ref{sec:application-synthesis}.

\subsection{Decidim}\label{sec:application-decidim}

Decidim is a modular monolith built with Ruby on Rails. The codebase at \url{github.com/decidim/decidim} contains 27 first-party engines packaged as standalone Ruby gems at SHA \texttt{be39c244} (2026-05-12).

\paragraph{Software structure.}
The 27 engines fall into four groups: \texttt{decidim-core} as the foundational dependency, three infrastructure engines (\texttt{admin}, \texttt{api}, \texttt{system}), three shared-service engines (\texttt{comments}, \texttt{forms}, \texttt{verifications}), four participatory spaces (\texttt{processes}, \texttt{assemblies}, \texttt{conferences}, \texttt{initiatives}), and 13 components that attach to those spaces. Every engine except \texttt{decidim-core} declares \texttt{add\_dependency "decidim-core"} in its gemspec, matching a hub-and-spoke pattern. But 13 of the 27 engines have runtime dependencies on other Decidim engines besides core: \texttt{decidim-comments} has 6 user-facing dependents and \texttt{decidim-forms} has 5, making them Layer-2 hubs of the platform (Figure~\ref{fig:dependency}).

Components are registered through a manifest API (\texttt{Decidim.register\_component}); third-party modules use the same interface as first-party ones.

\paragraph{Organisational structure.}
The Decidim Free Software Association was constituted on 16 February 2019 in a community assembly that approved its statutes. The Association holds the source code and trademark, transferred from Barcelona City Council in 2017. Its governance includes a General Assembly as sovereign body and four standing committees: Coordination, Research, Decidim Politics, and Public Institutions. The Public Institutions Committee brings Barcelona City Council, the Generalitat de Catalunya, the Barcelona Provincial Council, Localret, and other public bodies into the coordination structure \citep{barandiaran2024decidim}.

MetaDecidim is the community layer: a public Decidim instance at meta.decidim.org through which both governance and product work happen. The Decidim Social Contract, binding on members, commits signatories to AGPLv3 licensing, transparency, and ``equal opportunities for participatory objects'' --- the no-ranking-bias rule that surfaces in the software as a randomised default sort. The Association runs a small technical office (three employees per Barandiaran et al.) that handles release management and code review. Module ownership is distributed across Codegram engineers, Association staff, and the wider contributor base; 183 unique authors are visible in the commit log.

\paragraph{Business structure.}
The Association is funded through annual transfers from Barcelona City Council and the Generalitat de Catalunya under a formal collaboration agreement, with additional support from the Consorci Localret and the Barcelona Provincial Council \citep{barandiaran2024decidim}. Around this institutional core sits a network of anchor consultancies that fund themselves through municipal contracts: Codegram (Barcelona), Octree (Geneva), Open Source Politics (Paris), and smaller partners. The economic loop is: municipalities procure deployments from these consultancies; the consultancies are paid for installation, customisation, hosting, and bespoke modules; some of the work can flow back upstream as gem-based community modules or core pull requests. Decidim documents more than 80 community-developed gems, a visible artefact of this return flow.

\paragraph{Quality attribute profile.}
\emph{Modifiability} is high. The engine architecture lets deployers add, remove, or replace components without touching core code; customisation happens through modules, not deployment forks. The 80+ community modules are evidence that this modifiability is used in practice.

\emph{Interoperability} is moderate. A GraphQL API exposes most participatory content, and the project's integration choices (OpenStreetMap, Matomo, Jitsi) are deliberately open-source. There are no cross-platform standards for civic tech that would let Decidim compose with other platforms at the data layer, so interoperability remains less developed than modifiability or configurability in this case.

\emph{Configurability} is high. The 4 spaces and 13 components produce many possible default configurations, each customisable through component-level settings without code changes (Figure~\ref{fig:matrix}). \citet{palacin2024configurations} document 25 distinct production configurations across studied deployments.

\emph{Transparency and auditability} is high at the architectural level. Decidim ships an immutable \texttt{ActionLog} model with a four-level visibility enum (private, admin-only, public-only, all) and an organisation scope. The \texttt{Traceable} concern adds PaperTrail-based version history to every changeable model, so per-proposal version diffs are available. Permission-gated controllers enforce admin-log access. The GraphQL API is designed to expose public participatory content while not exposing individual votes or admin actions. Sorting is deterministic and seeded for reproducibility within a session. These mechanisms make auditability a first-class architectural concern, although they do not by themselves prove that non-technical citizens can meaningfully use the audit surfaces in practice.

Figure~\ref{fig:decidim-engine-activity} plots per-engine commits in the 12 months before the snapshot. All 24 user-facing engines see activity, with three new engines (\texttt{ai}, \texttt{demographics}, \texttt{collaborative\_texts}) accumulating their first hundreds. The modular architecture is therefore matched by visible maintenance activity across the engine set; no user-facing engine is dormant in this snapshot.

\paragraph{Three-structures alignment.}
The three structures appear aligned. The modular software lets external contributors ship gems without negotiating commit access to the core repository; the Association coordinates contributions and runs keystone functions such as releases, code review, and integration; the consultancy network has a business model around incremental deployment and module work; and the Public Institutions Committee channels public-body requirements back upstream. When Barcelona pays for accessibility work, that work can land in \texttt{decidim-core} and become available to other municipalities, which is the commons logic Barandiaran et al. articulate. The 80+ community modules and the steady commit rate after 2019 are visible indicators of this alignment.


\subsection{Consul}\label{sec:application-consul}

Consul and Decidim share an origin in Spain's 15M/Indignados movement. Barcelona initially contributed to Consul but proposed modularising the codebase. The proposal was rejected; Barcelona rewrote from scratch, creating Decidim in 2016--2017 \citep{codegram-decidim}. The original repository has since been renamed from \texttt{consul/consul} to \texttt{consuldemocracy/consuldemocracy}. Recovery is pinned to SHA \texttt{3c3b5c4a} (2026-05-11).

\paragraph{Software structure.}
Consul is a single Rails application: no gem separation, no engine isolation. Features (proposals, debates, budgets, legislation, voting, polls) share the same codebase, the same \texttt{ApplicationController}, and one \texttt{config/routes.rb}. Models live in \texttt{app/models/} with functional subdirectories but no module-level namespace boundaries enforced. All tables sit in one schema. There is no \texttt{ARCHITECTURE.md}, and the technical documentation covers installation but not architecture.

Customisation uses a ``custom shadow folder'' pattern: to change how a budget investment works, a deployer creates a file at the same path under \texttt{app/models/custom/} that overrides the original. This reduces some file-level conflicts but does not provide module isolation. The documentation's ``Getting Started'' step~1 is ``Create your fork'', making deployment forks part of the expected customisation path.

\paragraph{Organisational structure.}
The Consul Democracy Foundation was founded in 2019 as a Dutch non-profit \citep{linaker2025consul}. It was set up specifically because Madrid's institutional backing for Consul collapsed after the May 2019 municipal election. The Foundation is very small: a Director, a Network Coordinator, a Tech Coordinator, and a three-person board. Crucially, the Foundation does not employ developers. \citet{linaker2025consul} report that maintenance relies on ``a technical core group dominated by Spanish service suppliers dating to Consul's early days.'' The community is held together through annual ConsulCon events, a Slack workspace of 700--800 users, and a two-tier Certified Tech Partner programme. There is no Decidim-style Social Contract, no Public Institutions Committee, and no MetaDecidim-equivalent space where deployers participate in governance.

\paragraph{Business structure.}
The Foundation is funded by a patchwork: a single municipal sponsor (the City of Munich at EUR~20{,}000 per year), EU project grants, private donations, and revenue contributions from Certified Tech Partners \citep{linaker2025consul}. The Foundation does not commission core development directly; it cannot afford to. Municipalities procure Consul deployments from certified suppliers, but the architecture means much customisation happens inside the deployer's own fork rather than as a returnable contribution. \citet{linaker2025consul} report that contributions ``come largely from certified service suppliers, with fewer contributions from local governments and NGOs'' and that ``many development features rarely merge back to the core codebase due to architectural barriers and fragmentation across installations.''

\paragraph{Fork divergence.}
We enumerated all forks of \texttt{consuldemocracy/consuldemocracy} via the GitHub API and computed each fork's divergence against upstream master. Of 1{,}030 enumerable forks, 750 ($73\%$) are unmodified ``stars in fork form''; 280 ($27\%$) have at least one commit ahead --- approximately the ``around 250 deployments'' figure previous reports cite \citep{ntari2025consul}. The distribution within those 280 is heavily skewed: most have fewer than ten commits ahead, 39 have 100 or more, and only 5 have 1{,}000 or more.

The most-diverged fork is \texttt{AyuntamientoMadrid/consul} --- Madrid itself, the city that built Consul. It sits 12{,}725 commits ahead of upstream and 11{,}540 behind, last pushed 2023-05-23. The originating city therefore maintained a separate copy that drifted far from upstream and was eventually abandoned. This does not prove that the architecture alone caused the divergence, but it shows that even the originator ended up using the fork pattern that the platform made easiest.

\paragraph{Quality attribute profile.}
\emph{Modifiability} is low. Customisation is expected to happen through forks and shadow-folder overrides rather than through isolated modules. Shadow folders reduce file-level conflicts but do not remove logical coupling to the upstream codebase. The 1{,}030 forks are the visible signature of this extension model, although not every fork represents a production deployment.

\emph{Interoperability} is moderate. The public GraphQL API at \texttt{/graphql} exposes participatory content including individual vote records, but the data model is monolithic and there are no cross-platform standards.

\emph{Configurability} is moderate. Several components (proposals, debates, budgets, voting) can be enabled or disabled per deployment, with phase- and quorum-level settings. The configuration is less rich than Decidim's space/component matrix.

\emph{Transparency and auditability} is moderate. Consul uses the off-the-shelf \texttt{audited} gem to track per-record changes, but the UI exposes audit history only for Budget Investments. A separate \texttt{Activity} model logs five specific moderation verbs (hide, block, restore, valuate, email). Both surfaces are admin-only. There is no Decidim-style organisation-scoped \texttt{ActionLog} with public visibility options.

\paragraph{Three-structures misalignment.}
The three structures appear misaligned. The problem is not monolithic architecture as such: a monolith can work well when it is matched by strong central maintenance and clear contribution pathways. The problem is the fit between Consul's monolithic extension model and its organisational and business structures. The software structure makes local adaptation easiest through forks; reintegrating that work would require a strong coordinating body with sustained development capacity; the small Foundation cannot provide that capacity; and the business structure does not reliably pay anyone to provide it. The architecture's costs therefore land on individual deployers, who absorb them by maintaining isolated forks. The misalignment shows up in the fork data: 1{,}030 forks, 750 unmodified copies, and 280 modified forks with limited evidence of upstream return.

Two pieces of evidence make this concrete. First, Madrid itself ended up in the same fork pattern as other deployers, even with the resources and motivation of the originating city. Second, the post-2019 collapse: when Ahora Madrid lost the mayoralty in May 2019, the upstream commit rate dropped roughly $80\%$ within a year. The Foundation was constituted that same year specifically to fill the institutional vacuum, but without core developers or significant funding it could coordinate the ecosystem more easily than it could maintain the core. The hedge here matters: the claim is not that the monolith caused the misalignment, but that this monolithic extension model required a coordinating capacity that the organisational reality could not provide.

This differentiation supports including transparency and auditability as a primary axis of the framework, while leaving open the separate question of how usable these mechanisms are for non-technical citizens.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Architecture as Governance in Practice}\label{sec:disc-gov}

The Decidim/Consul comparison shows how architectural decisions participate in governance in civic technology. Decidim's modular architecture did not only make the code flexible; it also made it possible for communities and service providers to shape the platform without first negotiating commit access to the core repository. An organisation can build a module, publish it as a gem, and have it work alongside the official engines. This is close to what \citet{schneider2021modular} call ``modular politics'': composable governance primitives implemented as software. The 80+ community modules are one visible artefact of this politics in action.

Consul's monolithic extension model made a different path easier. The fork-and-shadow pattern made isolated local adaptation more straightforward than upstream contribution: deployers could either accept the platform as-is or maintain a separate fork. \citet{schneider2024governable} argues that platform defaults encode governance; in Consul's case, the default extension path pulled local adaptation toward forked copies rather than shared modules. The pattern reached its most visible case in Consul's own originator: Madrid maintained a fork 12{,}725 commits ahead of upstream, last touched in May 2023. This does not prove that architecture alone caused the fragmentation, but it shows that even the originator ended up in the same divergence pattern visible across the wider deployer base.

The two platforms can also be read through \citet{iansiti2004strategy}'s ecosystem actor roles. Decidim's Association operates as a keystone, maintaining shared infrastructure while enabling third-party modules on top; the 80+ community modules are the niche-creation activity this keystone structure makes possible. Consul's Madrid team operated more like a dominator while the city's institutional backing held: it controlled the codebase, rejected the modularisation path proposed by Barcelona, and offered no equivalent module mechanism for external contribution. When the institutional backing collapsed in 2019, no successor keystone with comparable development capacity emerged. The vacuum was partly absorbed by deployers maintaining their own versions in forks.

\subsection{What SECO Research Gains}\label{sec:disc-seco}

The SECO health dimensions from \citet{jansen2014measuring} --- productivity, robustness, niche creation --- offer useful organising concepts for civic tech, but they do not by themselves distinguish ecosystem extension from silo formation. Consul's 2017--2019 peak generated thousands of commits and dozens of unique contributors --- numbers a productivity-only assessment might read as a thriving project. Yet the extension model pushed adopters toward separate forks, and when Ahora Madrid lost the city in 2019 the upstream commit rate dropped roughly $80\%$. By productivity measures, the ecosystem looked active; by fork-divergence measures, it was already vulnerable to fragmentation.

Fork-divergence shape as a niche-creation diagnostic (Section~\ref{sec:framework-fd}) is one concrete contribution this paper makes to SECO research. Figure~\ref{fig:consul-divergence} plots all 1{,}030 enumerable Consul forks. The ahead-only region --- forks that add local work while staying in sync with upstream --- is empty: not a single fork has stayed in sync while adding contributions. The mass sits in the behind-only region (750 unmodified forks falling behind) and the ahead-and-behind region (the deep silos, including Madrid). This L-shape is a fragmentation signal: the fork population extends away from upstream rather than around it. The shape is qualitative and visual, complementary to scalar measures used in the fork-mining literature.

The three-structures framework of \citet{christensen2014analysis}, applied here to civic tech, also extends the SECO toolkit. The misalignment reading of Consul's fork fragmentation makes the structural argument concrete: not that the architecture was simply bad, but that the extension model required a coordinating capacity the organisational and business structures could not provide. Architecture and organisation diverged regardless of which way the causal arrow runs.

\subsection{Implications for the Democracy Stack}\label{sec:disc-stack}

International IDEA \citeyearpar{idea2026democracy} calls for a democracy stack but leaves the architectural decisions implicit. \citet{ceps2025building} advocates for interoperable European digital public infrastructure but does not engage the modifiability-against-sustainability trade-off that shapes whether interoperable infrastructure can actually be adapted by the communities it serves. Two lessons from our cases speak to these gaps.

\paragraph{Modularity as a condition for pluralistic adaptation.}
Trade-off 1 (modifiability against sustainability burden) makes the strongest case for modular architecture in a democracy stack. The Decidim/Consul comparison shows what can happen on either side. The trade-off is real: modular maintenance is harder than maintaining a smaller central codebase. But the alternative, when local adaptation is needed and no other extension mechanism exists, is fork fragmentation that upstream cannot easily absorb. For a democracy stack that must accommodate diverse institutional contexts, modularity appears to be a central architectural condition for pluralistic adaptation. The sustainability burden should therefore be treated not as an implementation inconvenience, but as a governance cost that must be deliberately funded and coordinated.

\paragraph{Transparency by design, not by audit.}
Decidim's transparency and auditability come from architectural decisions made early: a first-class \texttt{ActionLog}, the \texttt{Traceable} concern wired into changeable models, and a GraphQL API designed to expose public participatory content while protecting sensitive data. Consul's transparency mechanisms are narrower and implemented mainly through off-the-shelf audit and activity-tracking features. For a democracy stack that has to remain auditable across deployments and over time, transparency is difficult to add as a superficial feature after launch; it has to be treated as an architectural property. The cost --- audit infrastructure across models, permissions, visibility levels, and APIs --- is real, but without it the legitimacy of participatory outcomes is harder to verify.

\subsection{Quality Attributes Not in the Framework}\label{sec:disc-missing}

Three quality attributes that matter for democratic infrastructure are not primary axes in our framework: security, accessibility, and sovereignty. We left them out because the Decidim/Consul comparison does not strongly differentiate the platforms along these axes. Both follow standard Rails security practices; both have accessibility statements and basic WCAG compliance work; both are AGPL-licensed and self-hostable. Engaging these QAs properly would need either different methods (threat modelling for security; user studies for accessibility) or different cases (a commercial SaaS for sovereignty; a federated or anonymous platform for security). They belong in a fuller framework. We name them here to be explicit about what our two cases can and cannot teach.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:evaluation}

The two-platform comparison is the framework's first worked application, not a validation across the field. Several limitations bound what we can claim.

\emph{Causality.} The architecture-as-governance reading is correlational. Barcelona and Madrid had different governance cultures and political trajectories; Madrid's Ahora Madrid government lost power in 2019, hurting Consul's institutional backing. Separating what the architecture caused from what politics caused would need interview research we did not do. The hedge is real: the architecture and the organisational reality diverged in opposite directions, and the misalignment matters regardless of which way the causal arrow runs.

\emph{Single coder.} The architectural and ecosystem judgements were made by one author; a second coder could disagree on edge cases (which engine counts as ``shared service'', which fork counts as ``substantively modified''). Where possible we triangulated against published characterisations --- \citet{linaker2025consul} on Consul's organisational structure, \citet{barandiaran2024decidim} on Decidim's governance, the OSOR and NTARI reports on Consul deployments --- and noted disagreements explicitly.

\emph{Anglophone bias.} Almost all our sources are English; Decidim has substantial Spanish- and Catalan-language documentation, and the Consul fork landscape is heavily Spanish, Catalan, and Latin American. We likely missed material that would refine our reading.

\emph{Selection on the dependent variable.} We chose two platforms that exist; the civic tech graveyard --- platforms that never reached production or failed invisibly --- is invisible to this study. The trade-offs we identify are conditional on survival.

\emph{Architectural recovery limits.} Architectural recovery captures dependency structure and extension mechanisms, not runtime behaviour. Evolutionary metrics use email-based author identity, which conflates authors with multiple emails. Fork analysis covers public forks only and may miss private production deployments. Each of these is a real limit on what the methods can see.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Decidim and Consul started from similar political and historical origins but developed into different software ecosystems. Decidim's modular software appears to fit its coordinating Association and consultancy-anchored funding base. Consul's monolithic extension model appears less well matched to its small Foundation and shoestring funding, and this misalignment is visible in the platform's fork fragmentation. This paper makes sense of that contrast by bringing together three established literatures: software architecture quality attributes, \citet{christensen2014analysis}'s three-structures lens for software ecosystems, and the architecture-as-governance thesis from STS and platform studies. The contribution is not to invent those components, but to combine them into an analytical framework for civic technology platforms and demonstrate how the framework reads two worked cases. The main methodological contribution that emerged from the application is \emph{fork-divergence shape}: a diagnostic for whether a civic-tech ecosystem extends around a shared upstream or fragments into diverged forks.

We applied the framework to Decidim and Consul: two civic technology platforms with shared origins in Spain's 15M movement and different architectural paths. Decidim's modular software, coordinating Association, and consultancy-anchored funding form a structurally aligned ecosystem in this reading. Consul's monolithic extension model, weak coordinating body, and limited funding form a structurally misaligned ecosystem whose fragmentation is visible in fork divergence. The framework reads both cases consistently by asking not only what the software architecture looks like, but whether the organisational and business structures can sustain the architectural choices the platform has made.

This is a foundational step rather than a full validation. A fuller framework would need to examine quality attributes the Decidim/Consul comparison does not strongly differentiate --- security, accessibility, and sovereignty --- and would benefit from more cases that evaluate the framework against a wider design space.

\paragraph{Future research.}
Two directions invite follow-on work and could each ground a master's thesis. First, extending the framework to a wider set of platforms: CitizenLab to examine sovereignty under commercial SaaS models, Loomio to examine worker-cooperative governance, and a federated platform to examine security and identity. Second, stakeholder interviews with Decidim deployers, Consul fork maintainers, and Decidim Association members would complement the repository-mining done here with practitioner evidence and address the causal limitations in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}.
